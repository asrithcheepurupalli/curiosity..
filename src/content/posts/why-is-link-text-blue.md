## Observation

You read a sentence online and your eye snags on a single word that is a different color from the rest. Blue, usually, sometimes underlined. You know, without being told, that you can tap it and the page will change.

Nobody explained this to you. There was no lesson. The blue word simply meant "go," the way a green light means go, and you absorbed the rule so early that it stopped feeling like a rule at all.

It is strange that a whole world of links, across every language and device, agreed on one color. Why blue, of all the colors there are?

## Research

The honest answer is that no committee sat down and chose it. Blue links spread by accident, then hardened into habit. Early hypertext systems in the late 1980s experimented with various ways to mark a clickable word, and some of the earliest browsers ran on screens that could only show black and white, so links were simply boxed or underlined, not colored at all.

When color screens became common, browsers needed a default for the small set of things HTML left unstyled. Mosaic, the browser that put the web in front of ordinary people in 1993, rendered unvisited links in blue, and the browsers that followed, including the dominant ones, kept that default. Once millions of pages relied on it, changing it would have broken the one thing users already understood. The color stuck not because it was best, but because it was first and then everywhere.

There is a gentler reason it survived, though, and it has to do with the eye. On a white background filled with black text, blue is distinct without being loud. It carries enough contrast to stand apart from black, yet it is the darkest of the primary colors, so it stays legible as small text in a way that yellow or light green never could. Red was already spoken for by warnings and errors, and it can read as aggressive. Blue was available, calm, and clear.

It is worth hedging here: the precise reasoning inside those early teams is partly lost, and people who were there have offered different memories. What we can say with confidence is smaller. A default was needed, blue was chosen for reasons no one fully recorded, and the choice was good enough that the accident never had to be corrected.

## Reflection

*Some conventions are decisions and some are sediment. The blue link is sediment: a thing that settled into place before anyone thought to ask whether it should, and that now feels less like a choice than like a fact about the world.*

*We inherit countless agreements this way, signals we obey without remembering learning them. The wonder is not that the first answer was right. It is that being early, and being shared, can quietly do the work that being right was supposed to do.*
