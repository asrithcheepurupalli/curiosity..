## Observation

Stand in the middle of an open field on a windy day and listen carefully. The air is moving fast enough to push against your jacket, to lean your weight a little, and yet the wind itself is almost silent. There is no roar in the open. There is just pressure.

Then you walk toward the fence, or the bare tree, or the gap under a door, and the sound arrives. A whistle. A moan. A low howl that seems to belong to the storm.

The air did not get louder. It found an edge. Why does moving air stay quiet until it meets something to move past?

## Research

Sound is a vibration of the air, a pattern of pressure rising and falling fast enough for the ear to hear. Smooth, steady air sliding along is not vibrating. It is just flowing. A breeze, or even a strong wind crossing open ground, carries enormous energy and almost no sound, because nothing is making it stutter.

The noise begins when the flow is broken. When air is forced past a sharp edge or through a narrow gap, it cannot stay smooth. It peels off into swirls, small spinning eddies that shed one after another in a regular rhythm. Each shed eddy is a little pulse of pressure, and a steady train of pulses is, by definition, a tone. Physicists call these vortices, and the orderly procession of them downstream of an obstacle is a vortex street. A telephone wire in a gale hums for the same reason a reed does in a clarinet: the geometry sets the pace at which eddies break loose, and that pace sets the pitch.

The howl, then, is not the voice of the wind. It is the voice of the obstacle. The chimney, the keyhole, the bare branches, the eaves of the roof: each is an instrument, and the wind is only the breath. Change the wind speed and the pitch shifts, because faster flow sheds eddies faster. Change the gap and you change the note, which is why a storm finds a different song in every house it passes.

This is also why the loudest weather is rarely the most open. A blizzard across a flat plain can be strangely hushed, while the same wind through a forest or a city street fills the air with sound. The energy was always there. It only became audible where the world gave it edges to trip over.

## Reflection

*We tend to credit the force for the noise, the wind for the howl, the storm for its voice. But the force was silent. What we heard was the meeting place, the seam where moving energy was made to break against something fixed.*

*Perhaps that is true of more than weather. Power passing freely makes no sound. It is only at the edges, the gaps, the narrow places where it is forced to turn, that we finally hear it coming.*
