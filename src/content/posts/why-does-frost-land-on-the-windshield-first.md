## Observation

You walk out on the first cold morning of the year and the car is wearing a thin shroud of white. The windshield is feathered with frost, opaque enough to need scraping. You glance at the lawn a few feet away. The grass is wet, maybe, but green. Untouched.

The thermometer on the dashboard, if you had checked it, would have read a few degrees above freezing. The air never dropped to frost temperature. And yet here is frost, sitting only on the glass.

Two surfaces, the same air, the same night. One froze and one did not.

## Research

The answer is not in the air. It is in the sky. Every object warmer than absolute zero radiates heat as infrared light, and on a clear night that light streams upward into space with almost nothing to send it back. The night sky behaves like an enormous cold sink, its effective temperature far below the air near the ground. A surface facing it openly can shed heat faster than the air can resupply it, and so it cools below the air temperature. This is called radiative cooling, and it is why frost can form when the official reading never reached freezing.

Glass is unusually good at this. It is a strong emitter in the infrared, it is thin, and it holds little heat of its own, so it gives up warmth quickly. The windshield also tilts toward the open sky, exposing its whole face to that cold expanse with no shelter above it. Grass radiates too, but each blade sees its neighbors as much as it sees the sky, and the soil beneath holds a reservoir of stored daytime warmth that keeps seeping upward. The lawn is insulated by its own crowdedness.

So the glass loses the race. It drops to the dew point, then past it, and the water vapor touching it does not merely condense but freezes, knitting directly into ice. The same effect leaves frost on a clear car roof but not under the carport, on the open field but not beneath the single tree. Anything with a roof over it, even a thin one, is spared, because something warmer than space is now looking back down at it.

Clouds end the whole phenomenon. A blanket of cloud is warm compared to deep space and reflects the infrared back to the ground, which is why frost rarely forms on overcast nights even when they feel just as cold.

## Reflection

*The glass is colder than the air around it, which seems impossible until you remember what it is facing. It is not losing heat to the world beside it. It is losing heat to the emptiness above.*

*We measure temperature as if it were a property of a place. Sometimes it is closer to a relationship, a question of what each thing happens to be looking at, and how much of the open sky it dares to face alone.*
