## Observation

Every four years, February grows a day. We learn this early and trust it completely, the way we trust that a clock will keep its hands moving. Leap year arrives, the 29th appears, and nobody questions it.

Then someone mentions, almost as an aside, that the year 1900 was not a leap year. And 2100 will not be either. But 2000 was. The rule has a rule, and the rule has an exception, and the exception has an exception.

The question hides inside that small correction. Why does a system built to add days occasionally refuse to add one?

## Research

A year is the time the Earth takes to circle the sun, and that journey does not divide neatly into days. It runs roughly 365.2422 days. The leftover quarter is the reason for the leap year: skip it, and the calendar slides about a day off the seasons every four years, until eventually the equinox drifts into the wrong month.

Adding a day every four years treats the leftover as exactly 0.25. But the true figure is slightly less, closer to 0.2422. The fix overcorrects by a small amount, about three quarters of a day across four centuries. Left alone, that surplus would accumulate the way the original shortfall once did, only in the opposite direction.

So the calendar gives a little back. Century years (1700, 1800, 1900) drop their leap day to shed the excess. But that overcorrects again, removing slightly too much, so every fourth century year keeps its leap day after all. That is why 2000 had a February 29th and 1900 did not. Three skips, one reprieve, every four hundred years.

This is the Gregorian calendar, introduced in 1582 under Pope Gregory XIII to repair the older Julian system, which had assumed a flat 365.25 and had quietly drifted ten days off the sun. The new arithmetic brings the average calendar year to 365.2425, close enough that the gap with the real solar year takes thousands of years to matter.

## Reflection

*A correction is rarely a single act. It is a series of smaller corrections, each one overshooting the last, each one answered by something gentler. The calendar does not find the truth so much as circle it, tightening with every pass.*

*We tend to imagine accuracy as a fixed point we either reach or miss. More often it is this: a quiet ongoing argument between the measure and the thing measured, settled not once but again and again, a fraction at a time.*
