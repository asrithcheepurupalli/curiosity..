## Observation

The phone screen reads 7:42 AM. You glance at it, register that the day has begun, and look away. The letters do their job so well that you never read them as letters at all.

But pull them loose from the number and they turn strange. AM. PM. They are not words. They do not spell anything you would say aloud. They sit there, two capital letters each, asking nothing and explaining nothing.

So what are they short for, and why do we still type them on machines that have never owned a sundial?

## Research

They are Latin. AM stands for ante meridiem, meaning "before midday." PM stands for post meridiem, "after midday." The pivot point is the meridian, the imaginary line the sun crosses when it sits highest in the sky. Everything before that crossing is morning. Everything after is afternoon and evening.

The convention is old, older than mechanical clocks. Romans and later medieval Europeans tracked time against noon because noon was the one moment a sundial could mark with confidence. The shadow was shortest, the sun was overhead, and the day folded neatly into a before and an after. When clockfaces became common, the twelve-hour dial inherited that split, and the Latin tags came along for the ride.

What is worth noticing is the survival. Latin stopped being a spoken language of daily life centuries ago, yet these two abbreviations clung on inside timekeeping, a domain too practical and too universal to bother reinventing. They became technical shorthand, like the way scientists still write i.e. and e.g. without translating them. Most people who use AM and PM could not tell you the full phrase, and it does not matter. The meaning has migrated entirely into the abbreviation.

There is a small, honest wrinkle: noon and midnight themselves resist the system. Twelve o'clock is neither before nor after the meridian, it is the meridian, which is why 12 AM and 12 PM confuse nearly everyone. The Latin was built for a line, not for the points sitting exactly on it.

## Reflection

*A dead language is rarely entirely dead. It hides inside the things too useful to translate, riding along in the corners of clocks and prescriptions and legal forms, speaking through us while we believe we are only checking the time.*

*We think we left Latin behind. We carry a sliver of it in our pockets, glance at it dozens of times a day, and call it nothing more than telling morning from night.*
