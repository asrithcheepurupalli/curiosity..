## Observation

You tear a sheet of foil from the roll to cover a dish, and you notice, as everyone eventually does, that the two sides are not the same. One face catches the light like a mirror. The other is soft and grey, almost matte.

Somewhere along the way most of us decided this must mean something. The shiny side reflects, so perhaps it faces out for cooking, or in for keeping warm. Recipes hint at it. People argue about it gently in kitchens.

The question is small and almost embarrassing to ask aloud. Does the difference do anything at all, or is it simply a leftover from how the foil was born?

## Research

Aluminum foil is made by passing thick aluminum between heavy polished rollers, again and again, until it is squeezed thinner and thinner. By the final pass the metal is only a few thousandths of a millimeter thick, thin enough to tear if the machine pushed a single sheet through alone.

So it does not push a single sheet. In the last rolling step, two layers of foil are fed through together, stacked, sharing the strain. Each layer presses against a hard, mirror-bright roller on its outer face and against the other soft sheet of foil on its inner face. The side that meets the polished steel comes out shiny. The side that meets its twin comes out dull. Afterward the doubled sheet is separated and wound onto two rolls.

That is the whole secret. The shiny side is the side that touched the machine. The dull side is the side that touched another piece of foil. It is not a coating, not a treatment, not a choice made for your dinner. It is a fingerprint of the manufacturing, a record of what each surface was resting against in its final moment of being made.

For cooking the difference is negligible. The reflectivity of the two faces varies by only a small amount, far too little to matter to a potato. Manufacturers are clear about this: use whichever side you like. The exception is foil sold as non-stick, where one face is deliberately coated, and there the label, not the shine, tells you which way to turn it.

## Reflection

*We are quick to read intention into a difference. If two things are not the same, we assume someone meant them to be different, and we look for the purpose we are sure must be hidden there.*

*But some differences are only the trace of how a thing came to exist, the mark of what it pressed against on its way to becoming itself. The foil remembers its twin. It was simply never trying to tell us anything.*
