## Observation

In the top corner of an app sits a button made of three short horizontal lines. You press it without thinking, and a menu slides into view. The lines mean nothing in particular, yet everyone knows what they do.

No one taught you this. There was no lesson, no label, no moment where someone explained that three stacked lines should equal "more things, hidden here." You simply absorbed it, the way you absorbed which way a door opens.

The icon has a nickname, the hamburger, because the middle line looks like a patty between two buns. It is one of the most pressed shapes in the world. It is also one of the oldest, and for most of its life, nobody used it at all.

## Research

The mark was drawn in 1981 by Norm Cox, a designer working on the Xerox Star, one of the first computers built around a screen you pointed at rather than a line you typed into. The Star needed small pictures to stand for actions, and Cox needed something to represent a list of menu items. He drew three lines, deliberately plain, meant to resemble the very list it would summon. He later described wanting it to look like a "stop sign" for the eye, simple enough to read at a glance and not be mistaken for anything else.

Then it mostly disappeared. The Star sold poorly, and the interfaces that followed, through the 1990s and 2000s, had room for full words. Buttons said File and Edit and View. A drawing of a list was a solution to a problem that desktop screens, wide and generous, did not really have.

The shape waited about thirty years. What revived it was the phone. When screens shrank to the width of a hand, every word competed for space, and designers needed a way to fold a menu out of sight until it was wanted. The three lines were ideal: tiny, language independent, and shaped like the thing they opened. Early mobile apps adopted it, a 2009 redesign at one major company is often credited with spreading it, and within a few years it was everywhere.

It is worth being honest about the edges of this story. Cox is well documented as the author of the 1981 icon, but "first" claims in interface design are slippery, and the path from the Star to the modern phone was less a single line than a slow rediscovery by many hands.

## Reflection

*Some ideas are not early or late so much as waiting. The hamburger was drawn correctly the first time, then set down for a generation because the world did not yet have the constraint that made it necessary.*

*We tend to think good design solves the problem in front of it. Sometimes it solves a problem that has not arrived, and simply rests, fully formed, until the day the room finally gets small enough to need it.*
