## Observation

You lie back on the grass and look up at a clear sky. For a moment there is nothing but blue, and then you notice it: a faint, translucent squiggle drifting slowly across your vision. A thread, a cobweb, a tiny chain of beads.

You try to look at it directly, and it slips away, sliding off to the side as if it knows it is being watched. When you stop chasing it, it settles and begins its slow drift again.

It is not on the window. It is not in the air. It is inside your eye, and it has probably been there for years.

## Research

The space behind the lens of your eye is filled with a clear gel called the vitreous humour. When you are young, this gel is firm and almost perfectly transparent. Over time it begins to liquefy and shrink, and as it does, microscopic strands of collagen clump together and drift loose inside the fluid.

These clumps do not block light so much as bend it. They float between the incoming light and the retina at the back of your eye, and they cast small, soft shadows onto it. What you see is not the debris itself but the shadow it throws, which is why floaters look grey and out of focus rather than solid. The brain, which expects no shadows there, simply reports them as shapes adrift in the world.

The drifting motion has a quiet logic too. The strands are suspended in liquid, so when you move your eye to inspect one, it lags behind and then floats past the point you were aiming at. That is the slip you feel when you try to look straight at it. You are moving the camera, and the dust on the inside of the lens moves a beat later.

A blank, bright surface (a clear sky, a white wall, a lit page) reveals them best, because there is nothing else competing for the shadow. Most floaters are harmless and lifelong companions. A sudden shower of new ones, or flashes of light alongside them, is worth a doctor's attention, since it can mean the gel is pulling on the retina itself.

## Reflection

*We tend to think of seeing as a window: the world arrives, and the eye lets it in cleanly. But there is no clean window. There is gel and collagen and shadow, and a brain quietly editing most of it out so that the world can stay legible.*

*The floater is the rare moment when the apparatus shows itself. For a few seconds, looking at the sky, you are not seeing the sky at all. You are seeing the inside of your own eye, drifting between you and everything else.*
