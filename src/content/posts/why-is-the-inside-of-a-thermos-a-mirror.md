## Observation

You pour the coffee at seven, and at noon it is still warm. The flask sits on the desk all morning, doing nothing visible, asking nothing of you. It simply keeps.

Unscrew the inner cap and look down the throat of it. The wall is not white plastic or dull steel. It is silvered, faintly mirrored, catching a smear of your own face in the dark. Most people never look. The mirror is on the inside, where no one is meant to see it.

So why is it there? A mirror seems like a strange thing to hide inside a bottle.

## Research

Heat leaves a warm thing by three roads, and a good flask blocks all three. Conduction moves heat through touching matter. Convection carries it away in moving air or liquid. Radiation needs no medium at all: warmth simply leaves as infrared light, the way the sun reaches you across empty space.

The vacuum handles the first two. James Dewar, working in Glasgow in the 1890s on the problem of keeping gases cold, sealed a bottle inside a bottle and pumped the air from the gap between them. With almost no matter in that space, there is almost nothing to conduct heat across it and almost nothing to carry it by convection. Two of the three roads close.

But radiation slips through a vacuum freely. The warm inner wall keeps glowing its infrared outward, and the vacuum does nothing to stop the light. This is where the mirror earns its place. Dewar coated the facing walls with silver so that radiant heat, instead of crossing the gap, strikes the bright surface and reflects back toward the coffee it came from. A dull black wall would drink that warmth and pass it along. A mirror returns it to sender.

So the silvering is not decoration and not an accident of manufacture. It is the third defense, aimed squarely at the one form of heat loss a vacuum cannot touch. The flask wars on conduction, convection, and radiation at once, and the mirror is the leg that fights the light.

## Reflection

*We tend to think of a barrier as something that absorbs the blow, that stands and takes it. The mirror does the opposite. It defends by refusing to accept, by sending the energy back along the path it arrived on.*

*There is a kind of keeping that holds nothing in its hands. It only declines to let the warmth go, turning each departing thing gently around. The flask guards your coffee not by gripping it, but by giving it nowhere to leave.*
