## Observation

You cut an apple in half and set it down to find a knife or answer a message. When you come back, the white face has gone the color of weak tea, soft brown spreading from the cut edge inward.

It is still good to eat. Nothing has rotted in those few minutes. Yet the fruit looks tired, as if exposure to the room had aged it.

The strange part is what stops it. A drop of lemon juice, and that same surface stays pale for an hour. Why should breaking the fruit change its color at all, and why should a sour squeeze hold the change off?

## Research

The browning is a chemical reaction with a long name: enzymatic browning. Inside the cells of an apple, pear, banana, or potato sit two ingredients normally kept apart. One is a group of compounds called polyphenols. The other is an enzyme, polyphenol oxidase, often shortened to PPO. As long as the cell walls hold, these two stay in separate compartments and nothing happens.

The knife changes that. Cutting (or bruising, or biting) ruptures the cells and spills their contents together. Now the enzyme, the polyphenols, and the oxygen in the air all meet at the open surface. PPO uses that oxygen to convert the polyphenols into new compounds called quinones, which then link up into melanins, the same broad family of brown pigments that color human skin and hair. The brown you see is the fruit building pigment in real time, at the wound.

This is also, in a sense, the fruit defending itself. Those brown deposits and the reactive quinones along the way are thought to discourage microbes and insects at the site of damage, sealing a cut the way a scab seals one of ours.

Lemon juice interrupts the process at two points. It is acidic, and PPO works poorly in acid: drop the pH far enough and the enzyme nearly stops. Lemon also carries vitamin C, ascorbic acid, which acts as a sacrificial target. The oxygen and quinones react with the ascorbic acid first, converting the quinones back before they can darken, until the vitamin C is used up. Cold air slows the enzyme too, which is why a cut apple browns more slowly in the refrigerator, and blanching in hot water stops it entirely by destroying the enzyme outright.

## Reflection

*The fruit does not turn brown because it has been left alone. It turns brown because it has been opened. The color is not decay arriving from outside but a reaction that was always waiting inside, held in check only by the walls that kept its parts apart.*

*Maybe that is true of more than apples. Much of what looks like damage from the world is really something internal meeting air for the first time, and a little acid, a little cold, is sometimes all it takes to slow the darkening long enough to be seen as it was.*
