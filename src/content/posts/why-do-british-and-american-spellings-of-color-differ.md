## Observation

You type the word and the red underline appears, or it does not, depending on which side of an ocean your software thinks you live on. Color. Colour. The same idea, the same sound, one letter apart.

Nobody chooses this. The spelling arrives already decided, baked into the keyboard and the dictionary and the habits of a teacher you had at seven. It feels like weather, something that simply is.

But a missing letter is a strange kind of accident. Letters do not usually fall out of words on their own. Someone, somewhere, has to leave one out and mean it.

## Research

For most of its life the word was unsettled. English borrowed it from Old French as colour, then leaned on the Latin color, and for centuries writers used both without anyone keeping score. Spelling in general was loose. Shakespeare's name appears spelled a dozen ways. The idea that a word has one correct shape is younger than the word itself.

The split hardens in the early 1800s, and most of the credit, or blame, belongs to Noah Webster. Webster was an American lexicographer with a project larger than dictionaries. He believed a young republic needed a language of its own, a way of writing that did not bow to London. In his 1828 dictionary he trimmed what he saw as silent excess: colour to color, honour to honor, centre to center, traveller to traveler. The simpler forms, he argued, were more rational, easier to teach, and usefully American.

It would be tidy to say Webster invented these spellings, but the honest version is gentler. Several of his simpler forms already existed as variants, and he chose and championed them rather than conjuring them from nothing. What he supplied was not the letters but the will: a deliberate, published preference that schools and printers could adopt at scale. A standard is mostly a decision that enough people agree to repeat.

Britain, meanwhile, kept the older French-flavored endings, and Samuel Johnson's earlier dictionary had already helped set them in place. So the two spellings are not really right and wrong. They are two countries that standardized in different decades, each freezing the word at a slightly different moment, with Webster nudging one of them on purpose.

## Reflection

*It is easy to imagine that the way we spell, or speak, or measure, was handed down by some neutral authority. Often it was handed down by a person with an argument and a printing press.*

*A nation can be built in large gestures, flags and borders and anthems. It can also be built one quiet letter at a time, in a word so common nobody thinks to question why it looks the way it does.*
