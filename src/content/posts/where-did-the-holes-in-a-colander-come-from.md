## Observation

You tip the pot over the sink and a curtain of water falls through the bowl, steam rising, while the pasta stays behind. It takes a second, maybe two. You shake the colander once and set it down.

Look closer at the holes before the water comes. They are not random. They sit in rows, or rings, evenly spaced, each one a particular size. Someone decided this. A penne tube is about seven millimeters across, and the holes are smaller than that, but not by much.

Whoever drilled the first one had to answer a quiet question: how big, and how many, and how far apart?

## Research

Strainers are ancient. Bronze and pottery versions survive from Egypt and Rome, used for wine and broth long before pasta existed. For most of that history the hole pattern was whatever the maker could punch by hand, irregular and forgiving. The modern even grid arrives with stamped sheet metal in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, when a press could punch hundreds of identical holes at once.

The sizing is a balance with two opposing pulls. Drainage speed depends on open area, the total fraction of the bowl that is hole rather than metal. More open area means water leaves faster. You get that by making holes bigger, or packing them closer together. But every gain in open area is a step toward letting the food itself escape, so the hole has to stay reliably smaller than the smallest thing you want to keep.

So the holes settle into a comfortable middle, often three to five millimeters, dense enough to drain quickly, small enough to hold a grain of orzo or a lentil. The spacing matters too. Punch holes too close and the thin walls of metal between them buckle or tear under the press, weakening the bowl. The pattern is shaped as much by what the material can survive as by what the cook needs.

There is no single inventor to credit here, and probably never was. The colander we use is a slow average of many small adjustments, each maker nudging the holes a little bigger or a little tighter until the thing simply worked, and then everyone copied the thing that worked.

## Reflection

*Most good designs are not decided. They are converged upon, by many hands that never met, each solving the same small problem and quietly agreeing.*

*The holes are an argument that ended long ago, between speed and safety, and what we hold in our kitchen is the truce. We drain our pasta through a settled compromise and never feel the tension it resolves.*
