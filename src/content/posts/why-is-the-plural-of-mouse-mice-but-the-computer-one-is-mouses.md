## Observation

Two of them sit side by side on the desk, plugged into the same hub. Someone in the room says they need new mice. Someone else says mouses. Both pause, sensing that one of them is wrong and unable to say which.

The animal has had its plural settled for a thousand years. Mice is automatic, never questioned. But the smooth gray thing under your palm, named after the animal it vaguely resembles, refuses to follow. People reach for mice and it sounds faintly like a joke, as though the desk were infested.

So the same word, borrowed from itself, splits into two plurals depending on what it points at. The question is why the body remembers the old form and the gadget does not.

## Research

Mice is a fossil. It comes from a very old pattern in Germanic languages where the plural was made not by adding a sound but by changing the vowel inside the word, a process linguists call i-mutation. A suffix that once sat on the end pulled the vowel forward, then fell silent and disappeared, leaving only the altered vowel behind. The same machinery gave us foot and feet, goose and geese, man and men, louse and lice. These are not exceptions so much as survivors of a rule English used to run more widely.

That rule stopped being productive a long time ago. For roughly the last millennium, English has made new plurals one way: add an s. The vowel-change club is closed. No word coined after the Middle Ages gets admitted, no matter how much it sounds like a member. This is the deep reason. A new word, even a homonym of an ancient one, arrives into the modern system and takes the only plural the modern system still offers.

So when engineers in the 1960s looked at a small rolling device with a cord trailing behind it and called it a mouse, they had named a new thing. The word inherited the spelling and the image, but not the grammar. Its plural was decided not by the animal but by the date of its birth. Usage since has stayed genuinely split. Some style guides and many speakers accept mice for the device, others prefer mouses precisely to mark the difference, and the dictionaries record both without picking a winner. The honest summary is that the language has not finished deciding, and may never need to.

There is a quieter force at work too. Mouses keeps the two meanings apart. Saying we need three mouses signals at once that you mean hardware, not rodents, and language often tolerates a slightly awkward form when it does useful work.

## Reflection

*A word is not one thing but two: a sound and the moment it entered the world. The animal carries grammar from before the rules changed. The device carries the grammar of the day it was named, and the two will never quite agree, though they share a face.*

*We tend to think the old forms are the strong ones, hardened by centuries. But they are strong only in the words that already hold them. A new thing cannot reach back and borrow that age. It must take the plural of its own time, and live, a little awkwardly, with the resemblance.*
