## Observation

Someone across the room sneezes. Before they have finished, before either of you decides anything, the words are already out: "Bless you." You may not know the person. You may not believe in blessings. The phrase arrives anyway, on its own schedule.

It is a strange little courtesy. We do not say anything when a stranger coughs, or yawns, or hiccups. The sneeze alone earns this small spoken charm.

Where did it come from, this reflex we hand to people we will never see again?

## Research

The custom is old, and its exact birth is hard to pin down. The most repeated story sends it to sixth century Rome, where Pope Gregory the Great is said to have urged people to ask God's protection during an outbreak of plague. A sneeze could be the first sign of the sickness, so a blessing was a prayer pressed against a frightening symptom. The plague link is plausible but poorly documented, and historians treat the Gregory detail as tradition more than fact.

Older still are the superstitions that gather around the sneeze itself. Some peoples held that the soul might briefly leave the body in that violent exhale, and the blessing pulled it back, or barred the door against whatever might slip in while it was open. Others read the sneeze the other way, as something escaping: a small evil expelled, worth marking with a kind word. The Greeks and Romans sometimes treated a sneeze as an omen, a sign to be greeted rather than ignored.

What these explanations share is a sense that the sneeze is a threshold, a moment when the ordinary seal of the body briefly fails. The phrase was protection at that threshold. Other languages built their own versions of the same instinct. The German "Gesundheit," now common in English too, simply wishes health, skipping the theology and keeping the care.

The medicine that justified the fear is long gone. We know a sneeze is a reflex that clears the nose, not a leak in the soul. Yet the words stayed. They survived because they had quietly stopped being about plague or spirits and become about something simpler: a way to acknowledge another person in a small, involuntary moment.

## Reflection

*A custom can lose its entire reason and keep its whole shape. The fear that built this one has dissolved, and still the blessing comes, light and automatic, meaning only that someone noticed you.*

*Perhaps that is what outlives the reasons. Not the dread of the plague or the worry about the soul, but the impulse underneath them: to say something kind across a room, to a person mid-sneeze, just so they are not alone in it.*
