## Observation

At dinner, someone asks where you went to school, what you do, whether you own or rent. All of it is fair game. Then a newer colleague leans in and asks, lightly, what your salary is. The table goes still. Someone laughs to fill the gap.

Nothing was broken. No insult was offered. Yet a line was crossed, and everyone felt the same edge at the same moment, without ever being taught where it was.

What is it about that one number that turns an ordinary curiosity into a breach of manners?

## Research

The taboo is not as old or as universal as it feels. In medieval guilds and small towns, wages were often common knowledge, set by custom and visible to all. The idea that pay is a private matter belongs largely to the modern firm, where compensation became individually negotiated rather than fixed by trade or rank.

Once pay varied person to person, secrecy became useful to whoever did the paying. If two people doing the same work never compare numbers, neither learns they are underpaid, and neither has grounds to push back. Employers in many countries openly discouraged or forbade wage talk for decades. In the United States, that suppression was common enough that the National Labor Relations Act of 1935 protected most workers' right to discuss pay, a protection many people still do not know they have.

The etiquette did the rest of the work that policy could not. A rule enforced by embarrassment costs the employer nothing and reaches the dinner table, where no law applies. We learn to feel the awkwardness as our own delicacy, a sign of good breeding, rather than as a habit that happens to keep information from flowing toward the people it would most help. Studies of pay transparency tend to find the same pattern: when salaries become visible, gaps along lines of gender and race narrow, because what cannot be seen cannot be contested.

Privacy is the honest part of the story too. Money is tangled up with worth, with family history, with shame and pride, and few of us want our standing read off a single figure. The taboo protects that tenderness. It just also, quietly, protects the ledger.

## Reflection

*Some manners guard the vulnerable. Others guard an arrangement and dress themselves as kindness, so that keeping quiet feels like grace rather than compliance.*

*The useful question is not whether a rule feels polite, but who sleeps easier when it is obeyed. Follow the silence to its beneficiary, and the etiquette starts to look less like a courtesy and more like a fence.*
