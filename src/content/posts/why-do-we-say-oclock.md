## Observation

Someone asks the time and you answer without thinking: three o'clock, half past nine. The phrase rolls out smoothly, and the apostrophe in the middle never asks to be explained.

But it is a strange little mark. We do not say four o'minute or noon o'hour. The word arrives attached to the clock and nowhere else, a contraction left half-finished, as if a longer phrase had been worn down by centuries of use.

What was the rest of it, and why did anyone bother to say the clock at all, when surely the time was just the time?

## Research

The phrase is short for of the clock. In late medieval English you might say it is six of the clock, the way you would say a friend of the family. Spoken quickly, of the collapsed into the single sound o', and the longer phrase fossilized into the form we still use.

The reason for naming the clock is the more interesting part. For most of history, time was read off the sky. A sundial gave you the hour, and so did the church bell, the position of the sun, the length of a shadow. This was solar time, and it shifted with the seasons and your exact spot on the earth. When mechanical clocks began appearing on town towers in the fourteenth century, they offered something new: a steady, evenly divided hour that did not care whether the sun agreed.

The two often disagreed. A clock and a sundial in the same square could be off by a quarter hour or more depending on the day. So people specified. Saying six of the clock meant six according to this machine, as distinct from six by the dial or six by the sun. The phrase was a small act of bookkeeping, a way of naming your source.

We cannot date the exact moment the wording settled, and the spoken form leaves few records, so some of this is reconstruction rather than certainty. But the shape of the story is clear enough. The other phrasings (of the dial, of the sun) faded as mechanical clocks won, and only the winner kept its tag. We still announce, every time we name the hour, which authority we are trusting.

## Reflection

*A phrase like this is a receipt. It records a moment when two ways of measuring the world stood side by side, and we had to say out loud which one we meant.*

*The machine won so completely that the contest is invisible now. We say of the clock without remembering there was ever anything else to be of, the way a victory becomes most total when it stops sounding like a choice.*
