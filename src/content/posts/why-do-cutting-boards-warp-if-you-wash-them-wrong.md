## Observation

You leave a wooden cutting board in the sink after dinner, half submerged, the rest leaning dry against the basin. In the morning it has changed shape. The flat thing you owned yesterday now rocks on the counter, lifting at the corners like a slice of bread curling in a toaster.

Nothing tore. No one bent it. It simply sat in water overnight and decided to stop being flat.

The question is small but stubborn: why would soaking one side of a board make the whole thing bow?

## Research

Wood is not a solid in the way a brick is. It is a bundle of long tubes, the old plumbing of a tree, still arranged in the direction the trunk once grew. Those tubes are hungry for water even after the tree is gone. When you wet one face, the fibers there absorb moisture and swell sideways, expanding across the grain far more than along it.

So the wet side grows. The dry side does not. A board that swells on the bottom and stays put on the top has no choice but to curve, the larger face becoming the outside of a gentle arc. This is the same logic as a bimetallic strip in a thermostat, where two materials expand by different amounts and the mismatch forces a bend. Here the two materials are the same wood, separated only by how much water each side has drunk.

The effect is uneven because wood expands unevenly by design. It moves very little along the grain, more across it, and most in the direction the growth rings curve. A flat-sawn board, cut tangent to those rings, tends to cup away from the heart of the tree as it absorbs and releases moisture. Wash it on one side, dry it on one side, and you are pulling on exactly the axis that moves the most.

Much of this reverses if the board dries slowly and evenly, which is why the old advice holds: wash quickly, never soak, dry both faces, and stand the board on edge so air reaches all of it. The damage that lingers comes from repeated, lopsided wetting, the wood learning a curve through a hundred careless nights at the sink.

## Reflection

*A board does not warp from force. It warps from attention paid to one side and withheld from the other, the imbalance accumulating in silence until the shape itself gives the secret away.*

*Most of what bends in a life bends like this. Not from a blow, but from being soaked on one side and left dry on the other, for long enough that the curve becomes the new flat.*
