## Observation

A cumulus cloud drifts across a summer afternoon, white and slow and apparently weightless. It looks like something you could lift with one hand, a soft thing made of almost nothing.

Run the arithmetic and it stops being soft. A medium cloud holds enough water to weigh several hundred tons, the mass of a small herd of elephants suspended over your head.

So the question arrives quietly: how does all that water hang there, doing nothing, falling on no one?

## Research

The trick is in the size of the pieces. A cloud is not a body of water. It is a vast scattering of droplets, each one tiny, roughly a hundredth of a millimeter across, far too small to see on its own. The cloud is the crowd, not any single member of it.

Small things fall slowly. A large raindrop drops at several meters per second, but a cloud droplet drifts down at perhaps a centimeter per second, slowed by the friction of the air it has to push through. At that pace it could take hours to descend the height of a single room, and the air is rarely still for that long.

That is the second half of the answer. Clouds form where warm air rises, and rising air is the rule wherever a cloud already exists. The droplets are gently falling, but the air beneath them is moving up faster than they sink. The net result is that they hover, or even climb, like dust held aloft in a sunbeam by the slow convection of a warm room.

The whole thing stays a cloud only while this balance holds. When droplets collide and merge into drops large enough that the rising air can no longer carry them, they fall in earnest, and we call that rain. The cloud does not break. It simply lets go of the water it could no longer keep small.

## Reflection

*Weight, it turns out, is not the only thing that decides whether something falls. How a mass is divided matters just as much as how much of it there is. The same water that would crush you as a wave can hang above you as a haze.*

*Perhaps that is the quiet lesson of a floating cloud: that an enormous burden, broken finely enough and met by the gentlest upward push, can be carried as if it were nothing at all.*
