## Observation

You glance at a clock. The hour rolls over and the minutes climb, fifty-eight, fifty-nine, and then, just when you expect sixty, the number folds back to zero and the hour ticks up by one.

Everything else in modern life counts by tens. Money, distance, weight, the pages of a book. But time refuses. A minute holds sixty seconds, an hour holds sixty minutes, and nobody around you seems to find this strange.

It is the one place we still count the way people counted four thousand years ago, and we do it without noticing.

## Research

The number comes down to us from ancient Mesopotamia, where Babylonian astronomers and merchants used a base-60 system known as sexagesimal. We are not entirely certain why they settled on sixty, and honest accounts admit this. One plausible story is that they counted the three joints on each of the four fingers of one hand, twelve, and tracked groups of twelve with the five fingers of the other hand, giving sixty.

What is clearer is why sixty proved so durable. It is a remarkably cooperative number. It divides evenly into halves, thirds, quarters, fifths, sixths, and tenths. Before decimals existed, before anyone wrote 0.25, this mattered enormously. A baker splitting an hour or a surveyor splitting a field could take a third of sixty (twenty) without any awkward remainder. A third of a hundred refuses to come out clean.

The Babylonians used this system for astronomy, slicing the circle of the sky into 360 degrees, six neat sixties. The Greeks inherited it, then Ptolemy formalized the practice of subdividing each degree into sixty smaller parts, the partes minutae primae (the first small parts) and the partes minutae secundae (the second small parts). Those Latin phrases are where we get the words minute and second.

Decimal timekeeping has been tried. France attempted it during the Revolution, with ten-hour days and hundred-minute hours. It was technically clean and it failed almost immediately, because sixty was already wound too deeply into clocks, habits, and the human sense of an hour to pry loose.

## Reflection

*A hundred would have been simpler, and we chose against it without ever choosing. The clock on the wall keeps faith with people who watched a sky we have mostly stopped looking at.*

*Perhaps the things that last are not the ones built for elegance, but the ones built to be divided, shared, and handed forward without breaking.*
