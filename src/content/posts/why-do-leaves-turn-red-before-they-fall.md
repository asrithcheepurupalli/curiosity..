## Observation

A maple at the end of a street goes red in the second week of October. Not all at once. The crown lights first, then the color works down, until the whole tree stands like a struck match against a gray sky.

We tend to read the red as a kind of ending, the leaf using up the last of itself before it lets go. It looks like exhaustion made visible.

But yellow leaves and red leaves are not doing the same thing, and the difference is easy to miss. Why would a tree about to drop a leaf spend energy turning it a brand new color first?

## Research

Yellow is the simpler story. Those pigments, the carotenoids, were in the leaf all along, hidden under the green of chlorophyll. When the tree stops maintaining chlorophyll in autumn, the green drains away and the yellow that was already there is simply uncovered.

Red is different. Anthocyanins, the pigments behind red and purple leaves, are not waiting under the green. The tree manufactures them fresh, in autumn, at the very moment it is preparing to abandon the leaf. This is the puzzle: real metabolic effort spent on a part the tree is about to discard.

The leading explanation is that the red works as sunscreen. Before a leaf drops, the tree spends weeks pulling valuable nutrients, especially nitrogen, back out of it and into storage for next spring. That reclamation depends on machinery that is fragile in bright, cold light, which can damage the leaf and stall the salvage. A layer of red anthocyanin shades the working tissue, letting the tree finish emptying the leaf before the connection closes. The color is protective, not decorative, a tarp thrown over a building during demolition so the copper can be stripped first.

This is still an area of honest debate. Other researchers have proposed that the red is a signal to insects, a warning that the tree is well defended, and the truth may be some mix. But the sunscreen account fits a stubborn fact: red appears most strongly on bright, cold autumn days, exactly the conditions under which the salvage work is most at risk.

## Reflection

*We watched it as a last flourish, the leaf burning brightest before the end. It was closer to the opposite. The red is the tree being careful, drawing its resources home, dressing the leaf in shade so nothing useful is lost on the way out.*

*Perhaps that is what thoroughness looks like from a distance. Not a clinging to what is leaving, but a quiet, deliberate withdrawal, mistaken by us, every autumn, for a final blaze.*
