## Observation

Dinner is over, the bottle empty, and someone wets a finger and drags it slowly around the lip of a glass. Nothing, then nothing, then a thin clear tone climbs out of the rim and hangs in the room.

It is not a tap, which gives a single fading chime. It is a held note, steady as a bowed string, lasting as long as the finger keeps moving.

The hand is only sliding in a circle. Where does a sustained pitch come from in that small, smooth motion?

## Research

The trick is friction that refuses to stay smooth. A slightly wet finger does not glide cleanly over glass. It alternately catches and releases, grabbing the surface, dragging the rim a fraction with it, then slipping free as the pull overcomes its grip. This stick-slip cycle repeats hundreds of times a second.

A violin bow works the same way. The rosined hair grips the string, pulls it sideways, lets go, grips again. In both cases a steady motion in one direction is converted into a rapid back-and-forth tug, and that tug is what feeds energy into vibration.

The glass, for its part, has a natural frequency, the rate at which its rim wants to flex if you disturb it and leave it alone. That is the same note you hear when you flick it. When the stick-slip tugging happens to match that frequency, each little pull arrives in time to reinforce the rim's own motion, and the vibration grows instead of dying away. This is resonance: small pushes, well timed, adding up. The rim deforms into a slowly rotating wave, oval one instant, oval the other way the next, and the moving air carries it to you as a tone.

Pour in water and the pitch drops, because the liquid adds mass the rim must move, slowing its flex. Run the finger faster or press harder and the note does not rise; the glass holds its own pitch and simply sings louder, because you are pouring more energy into the same resonance. The frequency belongs to the glass. You only supply the timing.

## Reflection

*A circling finger has no pitch of its own. The note was already latent in the glass, waiting for a disturbance patient enough to keep arriving in step with it.*

*Perhaps that is what resonance asks of us everywhere: not force, but timing, the willingness to push gently and exactly when the other thing is already moving the way you hope.*
