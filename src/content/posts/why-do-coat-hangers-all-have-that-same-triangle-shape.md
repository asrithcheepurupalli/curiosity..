## Observation

Open almost any closet and the same shape repeats down the rod. A flat top that slopes gently from a central hook, then two arms that fall away and meet again at the bottom in a long straight bar. Wood, plastic, or bent wire, the silhouette barely changes.

Nobody chose this on purpose, exactly. You buy hangers in packs of ten and never think about them again.

But it is worth asking why a triangle, of all things, became the resting shape of a shirt. Why not a horizontal pole, or a peg, or a simple round loop?

## Research

The shape follows the body. A garment hung from a single point bunches and creases, with all the fabric dragging downward on one seam. Spread that same garment across a sloped bar and the load travels along the shoulder line instead, the strongest seam the garment has. The two arms of the triangle sit roughly where your own shoulders sit, so the cloth hangs the way it was cut to hang.

The downward slope matters too. Shoulders are not flat, and a hanger that drooped slightly at the ends keeps a coat from sliding off while letting it sit square. The bottom bar closes the frame and gives trousers or a skirt a second place to rest. So the triangle is less a design than a record of where a coat needs support.

The popular origin story belongs to Albert Parkhouse, a worker at the Timberlake Wire and Novelty Company in Jackson, Michigan, around 1903. The account, repeated in many tellings, has him arriving to find the coat hooks all taken, bending a length of wire into two oval loops with a hook on top, and hanging his coat on that. The company is said to have patented the idea, with Parkhouse seeing little of the profit.

How much of this is exact is hard to know. Hangers of various kinds existed earlier, and patents for shoulder-shaped supports appear through the late 1800s, so Parkhouse refined a shape more than he invented it from nothing. What the story gets right is the logic: the wire wanted to follow the shoulders, and once someone bent it that way, the form held.

## Reflection

*Most objects we call obvious were once a small problem someone solved at an inconvenient moment. The triangle in the closet is just the moment a coat met a wire and the wire gave way along the line of least strain.*

*We think of design as something added to the world. More often it is something the world was already asking for, waiting for a pair of hands to notice.*
