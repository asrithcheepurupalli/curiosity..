## Observation

Find an old parish register from the 1750s and turn to September. The entries run normally until the 2nd. Then the next line, in the same ink, the same hand, is dated the 14th. Nothing in between. No deaths recorded, no births, no weather. A week and a half simply does not appear.

Nobody slept through it. The days were not lost in the way an afternoon is lost. They were removed on purpose, by an Act of Parliament, and everyone went to bed on one date and woke up on another that should have been impossible.

The question is not where the days went. It is what happened to all the small ordinary things that had been hanging on them: a child due to turn seven that week, a rent owed on the quarter day, a man counting how old he was.

## Research

The Julian calendar, used in Britain since Roman times, assumed the year was a little longer than it really is. The error was tiny, about eleven minutes a year, but it had been accumulating since the fourth century. By 1752 the calendar had drifted eleven days out of step with the sun. The spring equinox was arriving on the page much later than it arrived in the sky.

Most of Catholic Europe had already corrected this in 1582, under Pope Gregory XIII. Protestant Britain, reluctant to follow Rome, waited another century and a half. When it finally acted, the Calendar Act of 1750 ordered that Wednesday the 2nd of September 1752 be followed directly by Thursday the 14th. The eleven days were not skipped over. They were declared never to have existed.

The renegotiation came next, and it was mostly clerical rather than dramatic. The Act stated plainly that nothing falling due in the missing window could be claimed early. Rents, leases, and debts were not to be paid until the natural number of days had actually passed, which pushed the old quarter day of the 25th of March to the 5th of April. That later shift is why the British tax year still begins on the 6th of April, a quiet fossil of the correction. As for birthdays and ages, people generally kept the date they were born on and simply lost a calendar week that year, the way you might lose an hour to a clock change.

There is a famous story that crowds rioted shouting "give us our eleven days," outraged at being robbed of life. Historians now treat this as mostly myth, drawn from a satirical election painting by Hogarth rather than from real unrest. The truer picture is duller and stranger: a population that mostly shrugged, adjusted its account books, and let the eleven days dissolve without complaint.

## Reflection

*We like to think dates are discovered, read off the turning of the world like a measurement. The eleven days reveal them for what they are: an agreement, kept only as long as everyone keeps it.*

*A man went to sleep a certain age and woke the same age, owing the same debts, loving the same people, while the number attached to the day had quietly changed underneath him. Nothing real was taken. Only the name we had agreed to give the morning.*
