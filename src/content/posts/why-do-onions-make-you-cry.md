## Observation

The knife goes down through a halved onion, and within a minute the eyes begin to sting. You blink, you turn your head, you breathe through your mouth. None of it helps for long.

It is a strange little betrayal. The onion sitting whole in the bowl did nothing. The same onion, cut, becomes something your body wants to weep away.

Nothing else in the kitchen behaves this way. You can dice a carrot in peace. So what is it about the onion that waits, intact and patient, until the moment it is opened?

## Research

An uncut onion is, chemically, holding two things apart. In one set of cells it stores certain sulfur compounds, amino acid sulfoxides drawn up from the soil. In another it keeps an enzyme called alliinase. As long as the cell walls are intact, the two never meet.

Cutting ruptures those walls. The enzyme floods into the sulfur compounds and goes to work, and the reaction produces an unstable intermediate that another enzyme (the lachrymatory factor synthase) converts into a small, volatile molecule: syn propanethial S oxide. This is the part that lifts off the board and drifts upward toward your face.

When that gas reaches the thin film of moisture on the surface of your eye, it dissolves and forms a mild sulfuric acid. Your cornea is densely supplied with sensory nerves, and it reads this as an irritant. The tear glands respond the only way they know, by flushing: a wash of water to dilute and carry the offender away. The tears are not sadness. They are rinse.

The defense almost certainly evolved against animals, not cooks. An onion underground cannot run, so it stores a chemical weapon that only arms itself when something bites or breaks it. We are simply large animals who break onions on purpose, several times a week, and accept the consequence.

Most kitchen tricks make sense once you see the mechanism. Chilling the onion slows the reaction and lowers how much gas escapes. A sharp knife crushes fewer cells than a dull one. Cutting near running water or under a fan gives the volatile molecule somewhere to go that is not your eyes.

## Reflection

*A thing can be entirely harmless and entirely capable of harm at once, the difference resting only in whether it has been opened. The onion keeps its two halves apart for a lifetime in the dark, dangerous only to whoever disturbs it.*

*Maybe that is the quieter lesson on the cutting board. What stings is rarely held in any single part. It waits in the meeting of things kept separate, released by the cut that brings them together.*
