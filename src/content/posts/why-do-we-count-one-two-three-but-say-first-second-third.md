## Observation

Counting is the first arithmetic anyone learns, and it sounds perfectly regular. One, two, three, four. The words march in a straight line.

Then you ask which one came first, and the line bends. First, second, third. Only at fourth does the pattern settle into something predictable, a number with a small tail attached: fifth, sixth, seventh, and onward forever.

The early ordinals do not match their cardinals at all. "First" shares nothing with "one." "Second" has no "two" in it. "Third" hides "three" only if you squint. Why do the first three behave like exceptions, while everything after them obeys a rule?

## Research

The tidy ending is the suffix "-th," and it is genuinely old, inherited from Proto-Indo-European through the Germanic line into English. For most numbers it does exactly one job: it turns a count into a rank. Six becomes sixth. The machine runs cleanly because these words, individually, are not spoken very often. No single high ordinal gets enough daily use to resist being regularized.

The lowest ordinals are different because they are everywhere. We name a first place, a second chance, a third try constantly, and words used that often tend to keep their old shapes rather than be smoothed into the general pattern. Linguists call this the effect of frequency: common words are repeated so faithfully that they preserve irregularities long after the regular rule has taken over the rest of the set.

"First" is a clear case. It does not come from "one" at all. It descends from a root meaning "foremost," related to "fore" and to the German vorderste, a superlative for what stands before everything else. The earliest thing was simply the front thing, named for its position rather than its count. "Second" arrived even later from Latin secundus, "following," borrowed into English to replace an older native word, "other," which had grown too vague to mark a clear rank.

"Third" is the one that kept its number while bending the sound. It does derive from "three," but the consonants swapped places over centuries, a slip called metathesis. Older English had forms closer to "thrid," and the d and r quietly traded seats until "third" felt natural. So the three smallest ordinals are irregular for three slightly different reasons: one borrowed from a notion of frontness, one borrowed from Latin, one scrambled in the mouth. The suffix took over only where attention thinned out.

## Reflection

*The words we reach for most are the ones we are least willing to tidy. A rule can reshape the rare and the formal, but the everyday resists, carrying its old face forward because we never pause long enough to file it down.*

*Regularity, it turns out, is what happens to the things we do not use much. The irregular survivors are simply the words that mattered enough, often enough, to stay exactly as they were.*
