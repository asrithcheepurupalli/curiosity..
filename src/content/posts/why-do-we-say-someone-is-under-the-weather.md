## Observation

A colleague calls in sick and says she is feeling a little under the weather. Nobody asks what she means. The phrase passes through the room and settles, soft and familiar, somewhere between a cough and an apology.

It is a strange thing to say, if you stop on it. Weather is above us. It is the sky doing its work overhead. To be under it is, in a sense, to be under everything, which is just where we are most of the time.

So why does this particular arrangement of words mean unwell? Why weather, and why under?

## Research

The most repeated explanation sends us to sea. On a sailing ship, the weather side was the side facing the wind, where the spray came hardest and the deck pitched worst. A sailor who felt ill, the story goes, was sent below, down into the hull where the motion was gentler and the air was still. He went under the weather rail, and then simply under the weather.

It is a clean account, and it fits the body. Seasickness is the original queasiness, the one that made people reach for a phrase. Going below deck really was the remedy: lower in the ship, closer to its center of gravity, a body feels the roll of the sea less violently.

Honesty asks for a caution here. Phrase origins are slippery, and the nautical reading is hard to nail to a first source. The expression appears in print in the nineteenth century, by which time it was already idiom, already worn smooth. Sailors may have coined it, or sailors may simply have been the people whose lives made it obvious, the way a fish does not invent the word for water but lives most plainly in it.

What we can say is that the logic holds regardless. Bad weather and bad health have always travelled together in English. Storms make people sick, cold makes people sick, and to be caught out in the open under a low and bruising sky is to be exposed to both at once. Under the weather names that exposure precisely: not struck down, just worn at the edges by something larger and moving overhead.

## Reflection

*There is a kind of illness that is not catastrophe, only diminishment. You are still standing. You are simply standing under something, the way a small boat sits under a sky it cannot argue with.*

*Perhaps that is the quiet wisdom the sailors left us. When the weather is too much, you do not fight it from the deck. You go below for a while, lower and stiller, and you wait for the sea to remember how to be calm.*
