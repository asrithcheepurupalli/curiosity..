## Observation

A runner glances at the marker. One mile. The number underneath reads 5,280 feet, and for a moment the strangeness lands. Everything else about distance seems to want round numbers. The metric world counts in tens and behaves itself.

Five thousand two hundred and eighty is not a number anyone would choose. It is not a thousand of anything, not a clean multiple of much. It feels less like a measurement and more like the remainder of an argument.

Which, more or less, is what it is.

## Research

The word "mile" comes from the Latin mille passus, a thousand paces. A Roman pace was two steps, left and right, and a thousand of them came to roughly 5,000 Roman feet. For a marching army measuring ground with its legs, this was sensible. The mile was a unit of walking.

England had a different unit it cared about more: the furlong, the length of a furrow a team of oxen could plow before needing to rest. A furlong was 660 feet, and farmland was laid out in furlongs and acres long before anyone tried to reconcile it with a Roman soldier's stride. The furlong was a unit of plowing.

The trouble is that 5,000 feet is not a whole number of furlongs. To make the two systems agree, you had to bend one of them. In 1593, an Act of Parliament under Elizabeth I did exactly that. It defined the statute mile as eight furlongs, which comes to 5,280 feet. The mile was stretched by 280 feet so it would land cleanly on the farmer's grid rather than the marcher's. The plow won, and the legion was quietly overruled.

Some of the older details are uncertain, and historians disagree about how much earlier the eight-furlong mile was already in informal use. But the shape of the story is clear enough: 5,280 is not a measurement of distance so much as a compromise between two ways of dividing up the world, one military, one agricultural, frozen at the point where they were forced to meet.

## Reflection

*We keep the awkward number because changing it would cost more than the awkwardness does. Every odometer, every road sign, every deed of land already agrees on it, and that agreement is worth more than tidiness.*

*So the mile carries a small fossil inside it: a Roman soldier and an English ox, neither of whom ever met, still negotiating the length of an afternoon's walk.*
