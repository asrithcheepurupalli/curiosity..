## Observation

A phone wakes you on Monday, and the word means something before you are even fully conscious. The light outside is the same light that fell on Sunday. Nothing about the sky has changed.

The day is a turn of the planet. The month is a rough nod to the moon. The year is a full lap around the sun. Each of these you can point to, look up, and verify. The week points to nothing. It is seven, and seven is just where it stops and starts again.

So why seven? And why does this one stubborn number, alone among our units of time, refuse to line up with anything we can see?

## Research

The trail runs back to Babylon, roughly three thousand years ago. Babylonian astronomers tracked seven moving lights against the fixed stars: the sun, the moon, and the five planets visible to the naked eye (Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn). Seven wanderers, seven days, one named for each. The names survived the journey into our languages, hiding in plain sight. Saturday still belongs to Saturn. In the Romance languages, Monday belongs to the moon and Friday to Venus.

There may also be a lunar ghost in the number. A lunar month of roughly twenty-nine and a half days breaks into four rough quarters of about seven days each, the span between new moon, first quarter, full, and last. But the fit is loose. Four sevens make twenty-eight, and the moon refuses to keep that schedule, so the week soon drifts free of the phases that may have suggested it.

That drift is the whole point. The week never quite divides the month, and it never quite divides the year. Three hundred sixty-five days leave a remainder of one, which is why your birthday lands on a different weekday each year. Precisely because the week answers to no celestial clock, nothing in the heavens can correct it or interrupt it. It just keeps counting, seven and seven and seven, indifferent to the seasons.

The Jewish Sabbath gave the seven-day cycle a religious spine and an unbroken practice, and Rome eventually adopted the planetary week across the empire. From there it spread until almost everyone on earth agreed, without any astronomical reason to, that today is Wednesday. Revolutionary France tried a ten-day week and failed. The Soviet Union tried five, then six, and gave up. The number that tracks nothing turned out to be the hardest to move.

## Reflection

*Most of our time we borrow from the sky, and the sky can be checked. The week we keep entirely among ourselves, a promise passed hand to hand for three thousand years, true only because we all keep saying it is.*

*Perhaps that is why it held. A thing anchored to the heavens can be argued with by the heavens. A thing anchored only to agreement can be broken by nothing but forgetting, and we have simply never agreed to forget.*
