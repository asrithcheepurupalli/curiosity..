## Observation

A full moon rises behind the trees at the end of the street, and it looks enormous. It hangs low and orange and close, the kind of moon people stop their cars for, the kind that ends up slightly blurry in a hundred phone photos.

An hour later it is overhead. Smaller now, cooler, ordinary. Nobody photographs it.

Nothing about the moon has changed in that hour. It is the same rock, the same distance away, give or take a sliver. So where did the giant go?

## Research

You can test this without instruments. Hold a small object at arm's length, an aspirin tablet or the eraser of a pencil, and cover the moon when it looks huge on the horizon. Then cover it again when it rides high. It fits behind the same object both times. The moon you saw as a giant and the moon you saw as a coin measure exactly the same. Photographs confirm it: the disc is the same width in pixels low or high.

So the swelling is not in the sky. It is in you. This is the moon illusion, and people have argued about its cause for at least two thousand years, since Aristotle wrote it down and got the explanation wrong. The honest position today is that there is no single agreed mechanism, only strong candidates.

The leading idea involves context. On the horizon the moon sits among things of known size: rooftops, hills, distant trees. Your visual system reads that distance and quietly resizes the moon to make sense of it, the same machinery that lets a coffee cup look the right size whether it is on the table or across the room. Overhead there is nothing to compare it to. Empty sky offers no scale, so the brain has nothing to inflate it against.

A related thread is that we perceive the sky itself as a flattened dome rather than a true half sphere, so the horizon feels farther than the point straight up. An object that occupies the same width but is judged farther away gets read as larger. The exact weighting of these factors is still debated, which is itself worth sitting with: a thing this common, this nightly, and we cannot fully say why it happens.

## Reflection

*It is humbling that the eye can be this confident and this wrong at once. The moon never grew. We did the growing, silently, on its behalf, and then believed the sky had done it.*

*Maybe that is the quiet lesson of the low moon. So much of what feels like the size of a thing is really the size of the room we put it in.*
