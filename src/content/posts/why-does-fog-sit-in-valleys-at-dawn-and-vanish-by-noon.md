## Observation

You leave the house early and the road ahead has gone soft. The valley is full to the brim with a pale, still fog, while the hilltops on either side stand clear in ordinary light. The mist seems to know exactly where to stop.

By the time you come back at noon, it is simply gone. No wind carried it off. There was no rain. The same low ground that held a lake of cloud now holds nothing but warm air and the smell of cut grass.

Why does fog settle into the lowest places overnight, and why does the middle of the day undo it so completely?

## Research

Air carries water as invisible vapor, and the amount it can hold depends on its temperature. Warm air holds a great deal. Cool air holds less. The temperature at which a given parcel of air becomes saturated, so that vapor begins turning back into tiny droplets, is called the dew point. Fog is simply a cloud that forms at ground level when air is cooled to its dew point.

After sunset, the ground stops receiving warmth and starts radiating its heat away into a clear sky. The air touching that cooling ground cools too, and cool air is denser than warm air. Being heavier, it slides downhill and gathers in the low spots, the valleys and hollows, the way water would. This is called cold air drainage, and it can leave a valley several degrees colder than the slopes just above it.

In that pooled, chilled air the temperature keeps falling through the night. When it reaches the dew point, the vapor condenses into the suspended droplets we see as fog. The hilltops stay clear because their warmer, drained-away air never crosses that threshold. The fog has a flat top because that is the level where the cold pool ends.

Then the sun returns. It does not burn the fog so much as reheat the ground, which warms the air from below. As that air climbs back above its dew point, the droplets evaporate again into vapor we cannot see. The lake of cloud does not drift away. It is reabsorbed, droplet by droplet, into the air that held it all along.

## Reflection

*The fog was never an arrival. It was the same air made briefly visible by the cold, and made invisible again by the warmth, the water present the whole time in both states.*

*Most of what surrounds us is like this, waiting just below the conditions that would let us notice it. The morning only lends us, for an hour or two, the chance to see what is always there.*
