## Observation

The room is quiet, a meeting maybe, or the held breath before a film begins. And then it arrives: a long, low gurgle from somewhere below your ribs, loud enough that you wonder if the person beside you heard it.

You assume it means you are hungry. Sometimes you are. But the strange thing is how often it happens when you are not, an hour after lunch, in the middle of a perfectly ordinary afternoon, with no appetite to speak of.

So what is making the noise, and why does it seem to ignore whether you actually want to eat?

## Research

The sound has an old, clinical name: borborygmus, a word that tries to imitate the thing it describes. It comes from gas and fluid being squeezed through the long muscular tube of your stomach and intestines as they contract. The contractions move in waves, and the waves are always happening, food or no food.

Between meals, those waves organize into something called the migrating motor complex, a slow housekeeping sweep that travels down the digestive tract roughly every ninety minutes to two hours. Its job is to clear out leftover debris, dead cells, and bacteria, pushing them along so nothing settles and stagnates. Some researchers describe it, fittingly, as the gut's cleaning crew, working the floor after the restaurant has closed.

When there is food present, the same churning is muffled. Solids and liquids absorb the noise, the way a full cupboard rattles less than an empty one. When the tract is empty, there is only gas and a little fluid to push, and nothing soft to dampen the echo. So the cleaning cycle that ran silently after lunch becomes audible by mid afternoon, not because you grew hungry, but because the muffling ran out.

Hunger does play a part. An empty stomach and the hormones that signal hunger can trigger stronger contractions, which is why the loudest growls often do coincide with appetite. But the rumble is not a hunger alarm so much as a sound that happens to be easier to hear when the room is empty.

## Reflection

*We tend to read the body's noises as messages addressed to us, a stomach asking for food, a sigh asking for rest. Often they are simply the sound of maintenance, work that was never meant to be heard, made audible only by the absence of everything that usually covers it.*

*Perhaps that is true of more than digestion. So much of what keeps us running goes on in the quiet, and we only notice it when the room, for once, has nothing else to say.*
