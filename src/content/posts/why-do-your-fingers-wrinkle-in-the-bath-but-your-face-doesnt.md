## Observation

You stay in the bath a little too long. When you finally lift a hand to reach for the towel, the pads of your fingers have gone soft and ridged, pleated like the surface of a dried plum. Your toes have done the same.

Your face, submerged just as long when you slid down to rinse your hair, is perfectly smooth. So is the skin on your forearm, your shin, the back of your hand.

The water touched all of it equally. Only some of it answered.

## Research

The old explanation, the one most of us absorbed without questioning, is that the outer skin simply soaks up water and swells. It is a tidy story, and it is wrong. If wrinkling were passive water-logging, every wet patch of skin would pucker, and the wrinkles would not arrive in such neat, repeatable patterns.

The clue came from medicine. Doctors noticed that fingers with nerve damage, where the supplying nerve had been cut, did not wrinkle at all when soaked. A purely physical swelling would not care about the state of the nerves. This one did. Wrinkling turned out to be an active response: when skin stays wet, the autonomic nervous system signals the small blood vessels beneath the fingertips to constrict. The skin above loses volume and the surface buckles into ridges, the way a deflating grape draws inward.

This only happens where you have the right kind of skin. Fingers and toes are covered in glabrous skin, the thick hairless surface found on palms and soles, densely packed with the nerves and vessels that make the response possible. Your face and forearm are not built that way, so they stay smooth no matter how long they soak. The wrinkling is also slow to start, taking several minutes, which fits a deliberate reflex rather than instant absorption.

As for why a body would bother, the leading idea is grip. Studies have found that pruney fingers handle wet objects more efficiently, the ridges working like the tread on a tire or the channels cut into rain treads, letting water escape so the skin can press flat against a slick surface. The evidence is suggestive rather than settled, and not every experiment agrees, so the function remains a careful maybe. What is clear is that the wrinkling is something your body chooses to do.

## Reflection

*We tend to think of the body as a thing that happens to us, weathered by water and time like a stone in a stream. Here is a small correction. The wrinkle is not damage. It is a decision, made quietly, below the level of anything you would call thought.*

*Perhaps the strangest comforts are these: that some part of you, without asking, is still preparing your hands to hold on.*
