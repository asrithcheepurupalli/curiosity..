## Observation

You turn the bottle over to read the year, or to see how much is left, and your thumb finds it: a cone pressed up into the base, a hollow that climbs into the glass like a small interior hill. The wine sits in a ring around it.

Most people register it as a way to cheat the eye, a trick to make the bottle look like it holds more than it does. That suspicion is so common it has its own folklore.

But the dent (its proper name is the punt) is older than the marketing it gets blamed for. It was solving a problem long before anyone thought to use it as theater. What was it for?

## Research

The honest answer begins with how glass bottles were made before machines. A glassblower would gather molten glass, blow and shape it, then snap it free from the blowpipe. That left a rough scar of glass on the base. A flat-bottomed bottle with a jagged seam would not stand level and might cut the table. So the maker pushed the base inward, tucking the scar up out of the way, where it could not wobble or scratch. The punt was, at first, simply tidiness.

Once it existed, it turned out to be useful in ways no one had planned. A dome resists internal pressure far better than a flat plane, which matters for sparkling wines whose corks strain against several atmospheres from inside. The same geometry that lets an arch hold up a bridge lets the punt hold back champagne. Structural engineers will tell you a curved surface distributes stress, while a flat one concentrates it at the corners.

The hollow earned other quiet jobs too. In an old red, sediment settles into the ring around the punt rather than clouding the last pour. Sommeliers slip a thumb into the dimple to pour one-handed with control. Bottles stack more steadily when the neck of one nests into the punt of another. None of this was the original reason, but a shape that works tends to keep finding work.

It is worth hedging here. Many bottles today are machine-made and could be flat without losing strength, so a deep punt is now partly convention, partly signaling, occasionally a way to fill a shelf with a grander-looking glass. The cynics are not entirely wrong about the modern bottle. They are just wrong about where the dent came from.

## Reflection

*A flaw in the method became a feature of the object, and the feature outlived the flaw. We kept the shape long after we forgot the wound it was hiding.*

*Maybe that is how most traditions begin: not as wisdom, but as the neatest way to handle a problem we no longer have, carried forward because it turned out to be good for other things we never meant it to be good for.*
