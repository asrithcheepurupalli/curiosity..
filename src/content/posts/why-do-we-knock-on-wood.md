## Observation

Someone says out loud that they have never broken a bone. A half-second later their knuckles find the nearest table, almost without permission. Knock knock. Nobody taught them the exact rule, yet they know it precisely: say something fortunate, and you must touch wood, or you have somehow put it at risk.

It is one of the few superstitions that survives in people who insist they hold no superstitions at all. We do it while laughing at ourselves for doing it. That contradiction is the interesting part.

## Research

The origins are genuinely uncertain, and anyone who tells you they know for sure is guessing. A few threads recur. Some trace it to old European beliefs that spirits lived inside trees, so touching wood was a way to call on their protection or to quietly thank them. Others tie it to later Christian practice, touching wood as a reference to the cross. There is even a tidy theory that it descends from a children's chasing game called "Tiggy Touchwood," where touching a tree made you safe, though the timeline makes that more likely a cousin than a parent.

What is clearer is why it persists, and that has little to do with trees. Psychologists who study superstition point to a simple human discomfort: we cannot stand the feeling that good fortune is fragile and entirely out of our hands. Saying the lucky thing out loud feels like daring the universe to take it back. The knock is a small action that hands us back a sense of control, a way to do *something* in a moment when there is nothing real to do.

There is a curious detail in the research. A few studies suggest the gesture works better when it pushes away from the body, like knocking down on a table or throwing salt, than when it pulls toward you. The motion of pushing bad luck away seems to soothe the mind more than a static touch. The body, it turns out, has its own grammar for "no, not that."

## Reflection

*A superstition is not really a belief about the world. It is a belief about ourselves, dressed up as one about the world. We do not truly think the table holds power. We think, somewhere underneath, that we cannot bear to feel powerless, and the knock is the smallest possible cure.*

*Maybe that is why it outlives our skepticism. The rational mind knows the wood does nothing. The older mind underneath simply wants a handle to hold while it walks past something it cannot control. We give it the handle, and we keep walking.*
