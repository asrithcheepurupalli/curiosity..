## Observation

A puddle sits in a dip in the pavement after rain. The next morning, on a cool grey day with no sun and no wind to speak of, it is smaller. By afternoon it is a dark stain, then nothing at all.

Nobody boiled it. The thermometer never climbed. The water simply left, quietly, while the sky stayed overcast and the air stayed mild.

It seems to break a rule we half remember from school: water turns to vapor at 100 degrees Celsius. The puddle was nowhere near that. So how did it go?

## Research

The rule about 100 degrees describes boiling, which is a particular event: the whole body of water reaches a temperature where bubbles of vapor can form anywhere inside it. Evaporation is a quieter, slower process, and it happens at almost any temperature, all the time.

The reason is that temperature is an average, not a verdict. The molecules in a puddle are not all moving at the same speed. They are a crowd, some sluggish, some quick, constantly colliding and trading energy. At any instant a few molecules near the surface happen to be moving fast enough to break free of the liquid and fly off into the air. The water does not need to be hot. It only needs a small minority of molecules that are, for a moment, energetic enough to escape.

Because the fastest molecules are the ones that leave, the puddle that remains is very slightly cooler, which is why evaporation feels chilly on wet skin. Conditions that help carry escaped molecules away (dry air, a breeze, more surface area for a shallow puddle) speed the process along. Humid, still air slows it, because molecules return to the water almost as fast as they leave.

So the puddle does not wait for heat. It is always losing molecules from the top of its energy crowd, and on an unremarkable day that slow leak is enough to empty it entirely. Boiling is the dramatic version of a thing that is happening, gently, the whole time.

## Reflection

*We tend to imagine change as an event that needs a trigger: a threshold crossed, a temperature reached, a moment when something finally happens. But the puddle teaches the opposite. It was leaving from the start, one fast molecule at a time, while everything looked still.*

*Most disappearances are like this. Not a sudden boil but a quiet evaporation, the energetic few slipping away unnoticed until one morning the dark stain is gone and we wonder when, exactly, it left.*
