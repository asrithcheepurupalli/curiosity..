## Observation

You step out of a cinema into the afternoon. The light hits, and before your eyes have even decided what to do, you sneeze. Not from dust, not from a cold. Just from the brightness.

Some people do this every time. Others have never once felt it and find the whole thing slightly absurd. There is no warning, no irritation in the nose, only a sudden bright sky and a reflex that arrives uninvited.

It seems like the wrong cause for the effect. Why would the eyes, busy with light, have anything to say to the nose?

## Research

The reflex has a name: the photic sneeze reflex, sometimes recorded in older papers under the gentle acronym ACHOO (Autosomal Dominant Compelling Helio-Ophthalmic Outburst). Estimates vary, but roughly a quarter of people have it. It often runs in families, passed down as a dominant trait, which is why a parent and child may share the same odd little sneeze in the same doorway.

The leading explanation is a case of crossed wires. The trigeminal nerve is a large nerve that carries sensation from the face, including the lining of the nose, and it is what fires when something tickles you into a sneeze. The optic pathway, which carries the signal of sudden brightness, runs close by. The theory is that a strong burst of light activity spills over onto the trigeminal nerve, and the brain, receiving the signal, concludes that the nose has been irritated. So it sneezes, solving a problem that does not exist.

Honesty requires a hedge here. The exact wiring is not fully settled. Some studies point to the trigeminal crossover, others suggest the brain's visual processing centers are simply more excitable in people who have the reflex. It is one of those small human facts that science has described well and explained only partway.

What is clear is that it is harmless, ancient, and oddly specific. Aristotle wondered about it. So did Francis Bacon, who guessed (wrongly) that the sun warmed the nose and drew out moisture. The question is old. The answer is still arriving.

## Reflection

*A sneeze in sunlight is the body answering a question no one asked, sending a true signal down a slightly wrong wire. The machinery is not broken. It is just generous with its connections, willing to mistake one kind of brightness for another.*

*Perhaps that is most of what we are: signals that spill, meanings that cross, a nose that answers for the eyes. We like to think our reactions are reasoned. Some of them are only proximity, one nerve lying close to another in the dark.*
