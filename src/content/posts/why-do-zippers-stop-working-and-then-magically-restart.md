## Observation

You are halfway up a jacket when the zipper stops. Not stuck on something obvious, just stopped, the slider refusing to climb another inch. You tug. Nothing. You tug harder, which feels like the wrong idea and is.

So you back it down a little, wiggle it, and pull again. This time it glides. The jacket closes as if nothing happened.

Nothing about your technique improved in those three seconds. The zipper simply worked, then did not, then did again. What changed?

## Research

A zipper is two rows of small teeth, called the chain, and a single sliding piece, the slider, that does all the work. Inside the slider is a Y-shaped channel. When you pull up, the two separated rows enter the top arms of the Y, get steered toward each other, and lock together at the bottom. Pull down and the wedge runs in reverse, prying each pair of teeth apart. The teeth never change. The slider just decides, over and over, whether they meet or part.

A jam is almost always something foreign inside that channel. The usual culprit is a thread of lining or a fold of shirt that gets drawn into the Y along with the teeth. The slider cannot close over the obstruction, so it stalls. Pulling harder only drives the fabric deeper and wedges it tighter, which is why force is exactly the wrong instinct.

Backing the slider down works because it reverses the wedge. The teeth open, the channel widens at the trailing edge, and the trapped fabric is released back the way it came. When you pull up again on a clear path, the slider meshes the teeth normally and the jam is gone. The same logic covers a slider that has spread slightly from wear: its jaws have opened just enough to skip the teeth instead of locking them, and a gentle squeeze with pliers, or simply a slower pull, lets them catch again.

So the magic is a small mechanical truth. A zipper is not really a fastener so much as a converter, turning the straight-line motion of your hand into the lateral coupling of two rows. When it fails, the failure is local and physical, sitting in a channel a few millimeters wide. When it recovers, you have cleared that channel without ever seeing what was in it.

## Reflection

*Most things that seem to fix themselves are really problems we solved without noticing. The fabric was there, then it was not, and our hands did the quiet work while our attention stayed on the frustration.*

*It is a useful shape to hold onto. The jam is usually small, usually close, and rarely cured by force. You back off, you free what was caught, and you try the same motion again on a clearer path.*
