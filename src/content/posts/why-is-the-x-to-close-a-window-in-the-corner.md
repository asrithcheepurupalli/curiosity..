## Observation

You finish reading something and you want it gone. Without thinking, you throw the cursor toward the upper or far corner of the screen and click. The window closes. You did not aim, exactly. You flung.

The little X lives there, tucked into a corner, and you hit it with a carelessness you would never trust on a button in the middle of the screen. Somehow the hardest thing to lose, your whole window, is guarded by the easiest target to reach.

Why a corner? Why not somewhere safer, more deliberate, harder to brush by accident?

## Research

In 1954 a psychologist named Paul Fitts measured how long it takes a person to move to a target. The answer turned out to be predictable. The time depends on two things: how far away the target is, and how big it is. Far and small is slow. Near and large is fast. This relationship is now called Fitts's Law, and it became one of the quiet foundations of how screens are designed.

The interesting part is what happens at an edge. On a normal button, you have to stop your cursor precisely on top of it, which takes care, which takes time. But the edge of a screen is a wall. The pointer cannot go past it no matter how hard you push. So a target sitting against the edge is, in effect, infinitely tall. You can overshoot as much as you like and the wall catches you.

A corner is two walls at once. You can throw the cursor diagonally with full speed and no precision, and it lands in the only place it can land. In Fitts's terms the corner behaves like an enormous target, far larger than its pixels suggest. This is why menu bars sit at the top, why the start button sits at the very bottom, and why the close button is parked in a corner. The designers were not decorating. They were exploiting geometry.

The exact placement, top right on some systems, top left on others, is partly history and partly habit, and the conventions diverged early without anyone settling who was correct. But the logic underneath is the same everywhere. The most reachable real estate on a screen is its edges, and the most reachable point of all is where two edges meet.

## Reflection

*It is a small kindness, this physics of corners, designed so carefully that you never notice it working. The screen gives you a wall to lean on precisely where your hand is least careful.*

*Perhaps that is what good design quietly is: not the thing you admire, but the thing that catches you when you stop paying attention, and lets a careless gesture land exactly where it meant to go.*
