## Observation

The camera comes up, and someone says it before you can think. Cheese. A small chorus answers, and for a held second a row of people arranges its faces into the same expression, lips pulled wide, teeth showing, eyes a little tense with the effort.

Then the shutter clicks and the faces collapse back into themselves. The word did its work and is forgotten.

It is a strange instruction, when you notice it. We are told to name a dairy product in order to look happy. Why that word, and why do we still obey it?

## Research

The mechanics are simple. The long "ee" sound forces the corners of the mouth back and up. It does most of the visible labor of a smile without requiring you to actually feel one. Say it slowly and you can feel your face being shaped by the vowel rather than by any mood.

The phrase seems to have settled into English in the mid twentieth century. One often repeated account credits a 1940s newspaper interview in which a politician shared his trick for good photographs, though the exact origin is hard to pin down and probably had several quiet beginnings at once. What is clearer is the contrast it replaced. Early portrait subjects were told the opposite: say "prunes," to keep the mouth small and composed. A wide smile, for a long time, read as foolish or unguarded, the look of a drunk or a child.

There was also a technical reason for all that stillness. Exposures in the earliest photography could run from many seconds to minutes, and a held smile becomes a grimace, then a blur. People sat braced and solemn because the equipment demanded it, and the solemn face became the look of a serious portrait. Faster film and faster shutters slowly removed the constraint, and only then could the casual smile move in to fill the space.

So "cheese" arrives at a precise hinge. The technology had finally made the grin possible, and a tiny piece of language stepped in to make it automatic. A century of stiff, closed mouths gave way to open ones, partly because a word was easier to follow than an emotion.

## Reflection

*We think the smile in a photograph is a record of how we felt. Often it is a record of a sound we made on cue, a vowel borrowed to do the job that feeling could not be relied on to do.*

*Maybe that is the quiet bargain of being photographed. We agree to perform the inside from the outside, to build the expression first and trust that something true will be caught inside the shape of it.*
