## Observation

You scoop a glass of seawater from the side of the boat. Held up to the sun, it is clear, faintly green at most, indistinguishable from tap water. You could read a newspaper through it.

Then you look out at the body it came from, and it is a deep, saturated blue stretching to the horizon.

Same water. Same molecules. The glass tells you nothing, and the open sea tells you everything. Where does the blue come from, if it was not in the cup?

## Research

Water is not perfectly transparent. It absorbs light, just very gently, and it does not absorb all colors equally. Red light, at the long end of the visible spectrum, is taken up most readily. Orange and yellow follow. Blue, at the short end, passes through with the least resistance.

In a single glass, this preference is far too slight to notice. A few centimeters of water absorb a negligible amount of red, so nothing about the color reaches your eye. The bias is real but unpaid. It needs distance to collect.

Over many meters, the accounting changes. Sunlight entering the sea loses its reds within the first ten meters or so, then its yellows and greens deeper down. What survives the descent and scatters back up to your eye is mostly the blue that was never strongly absorbed in the first place. The ocean is not adding blue. It is subtracting everything else, slowly, across a depth no glass can hold.

This is partly why deep clear water reads blue while a shallow tropical lagoon over white sand reads turquoise: less depth, less subtraction. Coastal water often looks green or brown instead, because suspended sediment, algae, and dissolved organic matter scatter and absorb their own colors, overruling the faint preference of the water itself. The pure blue is the color of water left mostly alone, given room to be itself.

## Reflection

*Some properties of a thing are too small to see in any single instance. They are not hidden, exactly. They are simply not yet large enough, and only repetition at scale makes them appear.*

*A glass is honest about a moment. An ocean is honest about a tendency. Both are telling the truth, and the difference between them is only how much of the world you let the same small fact pass through.*
