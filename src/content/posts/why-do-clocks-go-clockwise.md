## Observation

Clockwise. The word itself contains the answer — or seems to. Clocks go that way because that's the direction clocks go. It's definitional, circular, and entirely unsatisfying.

But something decided that direction before clocks existed. The convention didn't arise from nothing. It was inherited from something older, something people spent centuries staring at before mechanical timekeeping existed at all.

## Research

The precursor to the clock was the sundial. And sundials — in the Northern Hemisphere — cast shadows that move in a very specific arc: from west, through north, to east. Watch a shadow over the course of a day in Rome or London, and it sweeps in the direction we now call clockwise.

Early mechanical clocks were built by craftsmen in Europe. When designing the motion of clock hands to mirror what people already understood as "the movement of time," they copied the sundial. The shadow moved that way. The hands should too.

```
Northern Hemisphere sundial shadow path:
Morning → shadow points west
Noon    → shadow points north (shortest)
Evening → shadow points east

Direction of travel: west → north → east = clockwise
```

In the Southern Hemisphere, the sun arcs in the opposite direction — from east, through north, to west — meaning shadows move what we'd call counterclockwise. There is some evidence that sundials used in ancient Egypt moved in what we now consider counterclockwise. But because mechanical clockmaking was dominated by Northern European craftsmen, Northern Hemisphere convention became the global standard.

Had clocks been perfected in Egypt or southern Africa, every clock on Earth might run the other way. "Clockwise" would mean the opposite.

## Reflection

*The direction of time — or at least our symbol for it — was fixed by the latitude of the people who first built its instruments.*

*There is no universal law that time moves clockwise. There is only the shadow of a stick in the ground, somewhere in medieval Europe, on a sunny afternoon.*
