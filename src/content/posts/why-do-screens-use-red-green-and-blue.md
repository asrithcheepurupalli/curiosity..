## Observation

Hold a magnifying glass to a phone showing a blank white page. The smooth white breaks apart. In its place sit tiny stripes of red, green, and blue, switched on together, pretending to be something they are not.

There is no white in the screen. There is no yellow, no orange, no pink stored anywhere inside it. Only those three colors, brightening and dimming.

It seems like a strange shortcut. The world is full of colors. Why would three be enough to fake all of them, and why exactly these three?

## Research

The answer lives in the eye, not the screen. At the back of your retina sit cone cells, the sensors that handle color, and you have only three kinds. One responds most strongly to long wavelengths (roughly the reddish end), one to medium wavelengths (greenish), one to short wavelengths (bluish). Every color you have ever seen arrives as a ratio between just these three signals.

This means your color vision is not a measurement of light. It is a comparison of three numbers. A pure yellow wavelength stimulates the red and green cones in a particular balance. If a screen lights its red and green dots in that same balance, the cones report the same three numbers, and your brain receives an identical message. The screen never produces yellow light. It produces a forgery the eye cannot tell apart from the real thing.

So engineers did not choose red, green, and blue to cover the rainbow. They chose them to address the three cones directly, one primary aimed near each sensor. Three dots, three channels, three replies. The system is built backward from the receiver, the way a key is cut to fit a lock rather than a door.

This also explains the system's limits. A screen can only mix colors that fall inside the triangle formed by its three primaries, which is why some deep cyans and vivid violets in nature simply cannot be shown. And it is worth being honest: the specific red, green, and blue used vary between displays and standards, tuned by trade-offs in brightness and manufacturing, not handed down as the one true set.

## Reflection

*The colors on the screen are not in the screen. They are agreements struck inside the eye, three sensors politely accepting a substitute and reporting it as the original.*

*We think of seeing as receiving the world. It is closer to translation, and translation can be faked, as long as you know exactly how few words the listener actually understands.*
