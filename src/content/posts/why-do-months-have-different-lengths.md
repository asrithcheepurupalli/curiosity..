## Observation

Someone counts on their knuckles to remember how many days are in November. The raised bones are thirty-one, the dips between them are thirty, and April lands in a valley while July sits on a peak.

It is a strange thing to need a mnemonic for at all. The year is a single smooth circle around the sun, yet we have chopped it into pieces of slightly different size and then memorized the irregularity by hand.

If the calendar were a clean machine, every month would be the same length. So why isn't it?

## Research

The first thing to set aside is the sky. There is no astronomical event that makes July longer than June. The Earth does not slow down in summer. The lengths are human decisions, layered over centuries, and the seams still show.

The Roman calendar began as a lunar one, tracking the moon's cycle of roughly 29.5 days. You cannot have half a day on a calendar, so months alternated between twenty-nine and thirty to average it out. The trouble is that twelve lunar months total about 354 days, which drifts away from the solar year of roughly 365. The seasons slowly slid out of place, and priests patched the gap by inserting an extra month whenever the mismatch grew embarrassing.

Julius Caesar ended the patching in 46 BC. On the advice of Egyptian astronomers, he abandoned the moon entirely and built a year of 365 days with a leap day every fourth year. The extra days from dropping the lunar scheme were sprinkled across the months, which is roughly where our 30 and 31 pattern comes from. There is a popular story that Augustus later stole a day from February to make his month, August, as long as Julius Caesar's July, so that he would not appear lesser. Historians treat this as a likely legend rather than fact, but it captures the real spirit of the thing: the lengths were shaped by status and convenience, not by nature.

February stayed the runt. In the old Roman year it came last, and the leftover days had to go somewhere, so the shortest month absorbed the shortfall and the leap day's correction. The pattern we memorize is the residue of a lunar calendar bullied into matching the sun, with imperial egos pressed into the soft parts.

## Reflection

*We assume the divisions of time are discoveries, that we found the months the way we found the stars. Mostly we invented them, then forgot we did, and now we teach the seams to our children as if they were laws.*

*The knuckles are honest in a way the calendar is not. They admit there is no reason, only a shape you have to learn by heart.*
