## Observation

Two people pass in a hallway. One says "how are you?" and the other says "good, you?" and the first says "good" and neither breaks stride. No information has changed hands. If the second person were running a fever or grieving, the script would not have noticed.

The question was asked. It was answered. And yet nobody actually inquired, and nobody actually replied.

We do this dozens of times a day, with cashiers and colleagues and strangers, and we are mildly thrown when someone answers honestly. So what is the sentence for, if not for its own words?

## Research

The anthropologist Bronislaw Malinowski gave this kind of talk a name in 1923: phatic communion. Studying island communities in the western Pacific, he noticed speech that existed not to convey ideas but to establish a bond, language used, as he put it, in the mere exchange of words. The content was beside the point. The contact was the point.

A greeting like "how are you?" is a small handshake made of sound. Its job is to signal that the channel is open, that you are seen, that we are on neutral and friendly terms. Linguists later sorted this work into categories: phrases that open a channel, hold it, and close it. "How are you?" opens. "Take care" closes. The words are containers we agree not to look inside.

Historically the phrase did once mean what it said. Older greetings were often direct wishes for the listener's health, which is why so many of them ("farewell," "goodbye" as a worn-down "God be with ye") are really blessings. Over centuries the meaning drained out and the social function stayed, the way a coin keeps circulating long after anyone weighs its metal. The exact path of "how are you?" is hard to trace precisely, but the pattern is well documented: an honest inquiry hardens into a ritual greeting once everyone knows the expected reply.

What makes it work is shared expectation. Both speakers know the form, so neither has to think. That frictionlessness is the feature. A greeting that demanded a real diagnosis would cost too much to use at scale, and most encounters do not need one. The empty question is efficient precisely because it is empty.

## Reflection

*We tend to grade speech by how much it tells us, and by that measure these phrases are failures, pure noise. But some words are not meant to carry cargo. They are meant to carry warmth across a small distance, to say only: I noticed you, and I mean you no harm.*

*Perhaps the most human sentences are the ones that mean almost nothing, and would be missed at once if they stopped.*
