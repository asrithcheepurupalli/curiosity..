## Observation

Open almost anything you read on a screen and the background is white. The letters are black, or near enough. The text sits in a column with margins on either side, the way a paragraph sits inside the borders of a printed page.

None of this is required. The screen is a grid of small lamps. It could throw any color at you, in any arrangement, with no edges at all. Light has no margins.

Yet we built it to look like paper. We even gave it the verbs of paper. We turn pages. We scroll. We file documents into folders. Why does a thing made of light keep impersonating a thing made of pulp?

## Research

The polite word for this is skeuomorphism: a design that keeps the form of an older object after the reason for that form is gone. The digital trash can, the camera shutter sound on a phone with no shutter, the leather stitching once drawn around calendar apps. The white page belongs to this family, though it is so old we stopped seeing it as a choice.

Part of the answer is simply that the people who built screens had spent their lives reading paper. Early word processors and the first graphical interfaces of the 1970s and 1980s borrowed the desktop wholesale, pages and all, because a familiar metaphor lets a newcomer guess what to do. You do not need a manual to understand a sheet you can write on. The metaphor was a teacher.

Part of it is the eye. Dark marks on a light field give crisp, high-contrast edges, and centuries of type designers tuned letterforms for exactly that arrangement, ink absorbing light rather than emitting it. On a glowing screen the relationship inverts: the white is now the part shining into you. This is why long white pages can feel harsh at night, and why dark modes exist as a quiet admission that the paper costume was never the only option.

And part of it is momentum. Scrolling itself is a compromise. Paper comes in discrete pages; a screen is a continuous window onto a longer surface, closer to an ancient scroll than to a book, which is why the word came back. We never fully decided whether the screen should be a page or a roll, so it became an awkward, useful both.

## Reflection

*A new medium rarely arrives speaking its own language. It borrows the accent of whatever came before, and wears that borrowed voice until it grows confident enough to drop it.*

*Maybe the white page is not a failure of imagination but a courtesy, a handrail held out across the gap between one way of reading and the next. We are still crossing. The light is patient enough to pretend, a little longer, to be ink.*
