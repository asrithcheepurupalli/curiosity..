## Observation

You are washing a glass when it starts: four bars of a chorus, then the same four bars, then the same four bars again. Not the verse, not the bridge, not the part you actually like. Just this one stretch, running on a loop you did not switch on.

And it always stops in the same place. Right at the edge of a phrase, where the next line should arrive, the tape rewinds instead and begins again from the top.

You could sing the rest if someone asked. So why does the loop never reach it on its own?

## Research

Researchers call these involuntary musical images, and the looping ones tend to share a shape. They are short, usually a single hook of a few seconds, and they almost always sit on an unfinished phrase. The brain, it seems, dislikes a melody left hanging mid-gesture.

This connects to an old idea from memory studies. In the 1920s the psychologist Bluma Zeigarnik noticed that people recall interrupted tasks better than completed ones. An open loop stays active. A closed one is filed away and goes quiet. A musical phrase that has set up an expectation, a rising line that wants to fall, a tension that wants release, is an open loop in sound. Your auditory memory keeps it warm, waiting to resolve it.

But here is the catch in the looping kind. The part that snags you is often the part you only half know. You have the contour of the hook, its rhythm and shape, but not the exact notes that follow. So the brain reaches the boundary of what it actually remembers, finds no resolution stored there, and does the only thing it can: it returns to the start, where the memory is solid, and runs the lap again. The stall is not laziness. It is the edge of the map.

Surveys suggest most people get earworms several times a week, and that they fade faster when the song is allowed to finish, hummed all the way through, or displaced by another tune. Chewing gum helps a little, oddly, perhaps by occupying the same inner machinery that rehearses sound. The origins of any single earworm are hard to trace, often a fragment heard in passing, half attended to, which may be exactly why it never got fully learned.

## Reflection

*A loop is what longing looks like when it has no words for itself. The mind is not stuck on the song. It is stuck on the small distance between what it remembers and what it wants to reach.*

*Maybe that is why the silence feels like relief when the phrase finally completes. The circle was never about the music. It was about the wish, held open, to arrive somewhere and be done.*
