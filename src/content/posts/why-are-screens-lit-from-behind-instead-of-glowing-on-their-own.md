## Observation

In a dark room, an old phone shows a black image, and the black is not black. It is a faint, even grey, a sheet of dim light pretending to be nothing. Hold a newer phone beside it and its black is a hole. The screen seems to end exactly where the dark begins.

Both are showing the same color. One is glowing at you through it. The other has simply switched off.

It is easy to assume a screen makes its own light, pixel by pixel, the way a candle makes its own flame. For most of the screens ever built, that was not true.

## Research

A liquid crystal display does not produce light. It filters it. Behind the glass sits a lamp, once a thin fluorescent tube, later a row of LEDs, that floods the whole panel from edge to edge. The liquid crystals are tiny shutters. Apply a voltage and they twist, letting more or less of that constant glow pass through a red, green, or blue filter to your eye.

This is why an LCD cannot truly go dark. The lamp is always on, because it has to be on everywhere at once for any part of the picture to exist. The shutters can close, but they leak. To show black, the panel asks light not to come through, and a little always does. That faint grey in the dark room is the backlight, refusing to fully disappear.

An OLED works the other way around. Each pixel is its own small lamp, an organic compound that emits light when current runs through it. There is no shared lamp behind the glass. To show black, a pixel is simply given no current, and it makes no light at all. Nothing leaks, because there is nothing on to leak.

The backlight was never a flaw so much as a compromise. Making millions of independent lamps that were bright, even, and cheap was hard for a long time, so engineers built one good lamp and a clever curtain in front of it. OLED did not improve the curtain. It removed the lamp and let each pixel decide, on its own, whether to exist.

## Reflection

*There is a quiet difference between hiding light and not making it. One spends energy to conceal, and the seam always shows. The other simply rests, and the darkness it offers is real because it is empty.*

*Maybe that is what darkness asks of anything. Not a thicker curtain over the glow, but the willingness to go unlit, and to let the absence be the whole of it.*
