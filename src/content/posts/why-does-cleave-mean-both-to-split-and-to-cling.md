## Observation

A butcher lifts a heavy blade and cleaves the carcass in two. On the next page of the same dictionary, two people cleave to each other, holding fast, refusing to be parted.

It is the same word. Spelled the same way, sounded the same way, meaning almost exactly the reverse of itself. To cleave is to split. To cleave is also to stick.

We rarely notice, because context quietly tells us which one we mean. But once you see it, the word looks impossible, like a key that opens a door and locks it with the same turn.

## Research

Words that mean their own opposite are called contronyms, or sometimes Janus words, after the Roman god who faced two ways at once. "Sanction," "dust," "bolt," "overlook." Most are small accidents, one meaning drifting until it crosses the other. "Cleave" is rarer. It was born double.

There were two separate verbs in Old English. One was *cleofan*, meaning to split or to divide, the ancestor of the cleaver and the cloven hoof. The other was *clifian* (also written *cleofian*), meaning to adhere or to stick, related to clay and to the cliff that clings to a coastline. Different vowels, different conjugations, different families entirely. They were never the same word. They only sounded like neighbors.

Over centuries of sound change, the two verbs eroded toward each other. English flattened many of its vowel distinctions, and the spellings settled. By the time printing fixed the language in place, both verbs had arrived at the same four letters, c-l-e-a-v-e, carrying their opposite cargoes as if nothing had happened. The split-word and the stick-word collapsed into one form, and English, never tidy, simply kept both.

So the contradiction is not really a contradiction. It is a collision. Two strangers wearing, by coincidence, the same coat. (Etymology is rarely this clean, and some details of the early spellings remain debated, but the two-root story is well attested.) The cling sense now survives mostly in older or biblical phrasing, while the splitting sense does the daily work.

## Reflection

*We expect a word to be one thing, a small sealed container of meaning. But a word is only a sound that survived, and survival makes strange company. Two lives can end up sharing a single body simply because the centuries wore them down to the same shape.*

*Perhaps that is the quieter truth: to hold something close and to break it open are not always as far apart as they look. The same hand does both. The same word always has.*
