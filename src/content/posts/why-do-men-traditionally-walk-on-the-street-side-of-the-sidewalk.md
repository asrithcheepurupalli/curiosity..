## Observation

Two people walk down a street together, and one of them drifts, almost without thinking, to the outside edge of the sidewalk, nearest the passing cars. It happens during a slight rearrangement at a corner, a step taken to keep the other person on the inside.

Often neither of them notices. The one on the outside could not say why they are there. It feels less like a choice than a setting, something installed earlier and left running.

So why does this habit exist, and why is it so often the man who ends up by the road?

## Research

The most repeated explanation is plainly protective. Whatever came off the street, mud thrown by carriage wheels, water sheeting from a gutter, a startled horse, met the person walking on the outside first. Placing yourself there meant taking the splash so your companion stayed clean and dry. In an era when a ruined dress was expensive and laundering was hard work, this was a real, material courtesy, not just a symbol.

There is an older and grimmer layer beneath the mud. In cities before modern plumbing, chamber pots were sometimes emptied from upper windows into the street, with a shouted warning and not much else. Walking on the building side put you under those windows. The street side, ironically, was the cleaner place to be. The custom may carry a faint memory of steering a companion away from the wall, though this part is hard to document and easy to romanticize.

The same position doubled as a kind of guard duty. The outside of the footpath was where threat lived: runaway cattle, careless drivers, a stranger stepping out of an alley. To take that side was to put a body between your companion and the road. Some trace the habit further back to men keeping a sword arm free toward the open street, though that lineage is more folklore than fact.

What is honest to say is that no single origin owns this gesture. Mud, sanitation, traffic, and the old logic of the protective escort all point the same direction, and a habit reinforced from several sides at once tends to outlive every one of its reasons.

## Reflection

*The carriages are gone, the windows no longer open onto the gutter, and still the body drifts outward toward the road. A reason can expire while the shape it pressed into us stays behind, doing its small work in the dark.*

*Perhaps that is what courtesy mostly is: an old fear, worn smooth by repetition, that we keep performing as tenderness long after we have forgotten what it was protecting against.*
