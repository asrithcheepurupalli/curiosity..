## Observation

You halve an apple and nothing happens. The blade goes through, the white flesh browns slowly in the light, and your face stays dry. You could cut a bowl of them without thinking about your eyes once.

Then you halve an onion. Within seconds something is wrong. The sting arrives before you notice it starting, a thin burn that gathers at the corners and brims over. You blink, you turn your head, you cut faster to be done.

Both are just plant matter under a knife. One of them fights back, and the other does not seem to care.

## Research

An intact onion holds nothing that would hurt you. Its weapon is built only at the moment of damage. Onion cells keep two things in separate compartments: sulfur compounds in one place, an enzyme called alliinase in another. The knife breaks the walls and lets them meet.

When they mix, a short chain of reactions produces a small, volatile molecule that drifts up into the air. Its formal name is syn-propanethial-S-oxide, and it is unusually good at finding your eyes. The wet surface of an eye is mostly water, and when this sulfur gas dissolves into that film it forms a mild acid. Your eyes register the irritation and do the one thing they know how to do, which is flood the surface with tears to rinse the offender away.

This is a defense, though not one aimed at you. The onion grows underground, where insects and grazing animals are the real threat. A compound that punishes the mouth and nose of anything that bites is a sensible thing for a buried bulb to evolve. We simply happen to be standing over it with a blade and an open face.

An apple has no such arrangement. Its cut flesh releases enzymes too, but they react with oxygen and the result is brown pigment, not airborne acid. The apple's chemistry stays on the cutting board. The onion's chemistry comes looking for you, and the difference between a stained slice and a stinging eye is mostly a matter of whether the reaction is content to sit still.

## Reflection

*It is strange to be wounded by a thing's last act of self-protection, to receive a defense meant for a creature you are not, in a place it never intended to reach.*

*Most of what hurts us was never aimed our way. We walk into the crossfire of old arrangements made long before we arrived, and we call it the onion's fault when really we are just close enough to feel something else trying to stay alive.*
