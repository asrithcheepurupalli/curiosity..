## Observation

You set an alarm for an event at 12 PM and pause, just for a second. Is that midday or midnight? You count on your fingers, or you give up and type 11:59 to be safe, or you write "12 noon" in the calendar so nobody else has to do the same arithmetic.

It is a strange hesitation for something so familiar. We read clocks all day without effort. Yet twice in every twenty-four hours, the labels we trust quietly stop working, and we are left squinting at a number we have known since childhood.

The question hides inside the abbreviation. AM and PM are supposed to tell us which half of the day we are in. So which half does noon belong to?

## Research

AM stands for ante meridiem, Latin for "before midday." PM stands for post meridiem, "after midday." The dividing line is not midnight or any neutral marker. It is noon itself, the meridian, the moment the sun crosses the highest point in its arc.

This is the root of the trouble. The whole system is defined around noon, which means noon cannot sit cleanly on either side of it. It is neither before midday nor after midday. It is midday. The instant we use to split the day is the one instant the split cannot describe.

By convention, 12 PM means noon and 12 AM means midnight, but the convention is doing real work to paper over a contradiction. Strictly, noon is neither, and the labels only settle the matter by agreement rather than by logic. The number 12 also runs backwards against the others. From 1 PM to 11 PM the hours climb sensibly, but 12 PM arrives before 1 PM, so the count briefly resets at the very moment the meaning flips. Two ambiguities meet in the same square of the clock.

This confuses more than tired sleepers. Scheduling guides, airline timetables, and standards bodies have all wrestled with it, and many simply refuse to use 12 AM or 12 PM at all. They write "noon" and "midnight," or they adopt the 24 hour clock, where 12:00 and 00:00 leave nothing to interpret. Even careful clock and watch makers have shipped products that handle the rollover oddly, because the boundary they are dividing falls exactly on the divider.

## Reflection

*Most boundaries are drawn through empty ground, a line between two things with room to spare on either side. This one is drawn straight through the landmark it was built to honor.*

*Perhaps that is why noon feels briefly slippery. We are asking a single moment to stand on both banks of a river that it is, in fact, the source of.*
