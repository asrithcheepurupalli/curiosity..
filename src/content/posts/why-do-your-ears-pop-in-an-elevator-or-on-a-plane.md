## Observation

The elevator starts to climb and somewhere around the fortieth floor the world goes slightly muffled. Voices flatten. The hum of the cab seems to recede behind a thin wall. Then, without warning, a small click in the head, and everything snaps back to full volume.

On a plane it is the same, only slower. Pressure builds behind the face during descent, a dull ache that nobody quite knows where to put. People yawn on purpose, chew gum, hold their nose and blow. Children, who have not yet learned the trick, simply cry.

We call it a pop, as if something burst. Nothing bursts. Something opens. The question is what, and why it waited so long to do it.

## Research

Behind each eardrum is a small sealed pocket of air, the middle ear. For the eardrum to move freely and pass sound along, the air pressure in that pocket has to match the pressure of the air outside. When the two are equal, the eardrum hangs slack and listens. When they differ, it gets stretched tight, like a drum tuned too high, and sound goes dull.

The job of keeping the two sides matched belongs to a narrow channel called the Eustachian tube, named for the sixteenth century anatomist Bartolomeo Eustachi. It runs from the middle ear down to the back of the throat, roughly an inch and a half long, and most of the time it stays closed. This is on purpose. A tube that stayed open would let you hear your own breathing and the wet machinery of your own swallowing, which would be unbearable.

As you rise, the air around you thins and its pressure drops, but the air trapped behind the eardrum stays at the old, higher pressure. The eardrum bulges outward and stiffens, and the world goes quiet. The Eustachian tube, being soft and collapsible, tends to vent in this direction fairly easily: the trapped air pushes the tube open from the inside and a little escapes. The pop is that sudden release, the tube snapping briefly open and the two pressures becoming one again.

Descent is harder, which is why planes hurt more than elevators. Now the outside pressure is rising and the trapped air is at the lower value, so the eardrum is pulled inward and the tube is pressed shut from the outside. It will not open on its own. You have to open it deliberately, and the muscles that do this are the same ones you use to swallow and yawn. That is the whole secret of the chewing gum and the forced yawn: you are not distracting yourself from the pressure, you are working the small muscle that pulls the tube open.

## Reflection

*Most of the body's quiet maintenance happens below the threshold of attention. The tube opens and closes hundreds of times a day with every swallow, keeping two columns of air in balance so faithfully that you only ever notice the work on the rare occasion it falls behind.*

*Perhaps that is what a pop really is: not a malfunction but a glimpse, the one moment the silent caretaker lets you hear it doing the thing it does all day for free.*
