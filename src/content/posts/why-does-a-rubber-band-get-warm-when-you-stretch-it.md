## Observation

Pull a rubber band taut and press it quickly to your upper lip. It is faintly warm. Let it snap back to its resting length, hold it there, and touch it again. Now it feels cool.

Most things grow warm when you compress them and cool when they relax, or they simply hold their temperature and ignore you. The rubber band does the opposite, and it does it without electricity, friction, or any obvious source of heat.

You have done no chemistry. You have only changed its shape. Where did the warmth come from, and where does it go when the band relaxes?

## Research

A rubber band is a tangle of long polymer chains, folded and coiled like dropped string. At rest, those chains are free to wander through countless random arrangements. In the language of thermodynamics, that disorder is high entropy, and it is the band's preferred state.

When you stretch the band, you force the chains to straighten and line up. There are far fewer ways to be straight than to be coiled, so you have lowered the entropy of the rubber. Nature resists this. To balance the books, the band sheds energy as heat, which is the warmth you feel on your lip. Let go, and the chains rush back into their tangled, high-entropy sprawl, pulling heat in from their surroundings to fund the return. That is the coolness.

This is why rubber is sometimes called an entropic spring. Steel resists stretching because you are prying its atoms apart against electrical bonds. Rubber resists because stretching it is statistically unlikely, and the pull you feel is mostly the chains' tendency to be disordered again. The force is made of probability more than of bonds.

The effect has a strange signature. Most materials expand when heated, but a stretched rubber band under tension contracts when warmed, because heating only deepens its hunger to recoil. John Gough noticed the warmth in 1805, and James Joule measured it carefully in the 1850s, which is why the behavior is often called the Gough to Joule effect. The detail that gives it weight: the heat is not a side effect of the stretching. The heat is the stretching, read out in another currency.

## Reflection

*We tend to think of warmth as something added, a fire lit, a current run. Here it is only bookkeeping made physical, the cost of order paid out loud.*

*Stretch the band and you make it briefly simpler than it wants to be, and it complains in heat. Release it, and it cools as it returns to its preferred confusion. There is something honest in a thing that warms when forced into line and breathes easy only when allowed to tangle again.*
